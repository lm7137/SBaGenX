1: Theory
---------

The basic idea of binaural beats is that by applying slightly
different frequency sine waves to the two ears, a beating affect is
created in the brain itself, due to the internal wiring of the brain.
If, in the presence of these tones, you relax and let your mind go,
your mind will naturally synchronize with the beat frequency.  In this
way it is possible to accurately lead the brain to various states,
according to the frequencies that you apply.

It is also possible to play several different tones together, giving
several different beat frequencies, and programming quite a complex
brain-state based on several frequencies (in several different bands).
These complex mixtures of frequencies are the basis of the Hemi-Sync
(TM) process from the Monroe Institute, based on Robert Monroe's
experiments with out-of-body experiences and so on.

Taking an alternative approach, CenterPointe <www.centerpointe.com>
use single tones, but vary the carrier frequencies to achieve a kind
of emotional cleansing effect, which is also an extremely valuable and
interesting technique.  There may well be other useful binaural beat
based techniques yet to be discovered.

For an overview of the theory of binaural beats and how they affect
the mind, take a look at the Monroe Institute site:
https://www.monroeinstitute.org

Note that the recommended way to listen to binaural beats is with
headphones.  Any other way causes some of the signal intended for the
left ear to reach the right ear and vice versa, which means that the
effect is less strong.  Also, in both the Monroe Institute and
CenterPointe programmes, the binaural tones are only just audible
above the pink noise or soundtrack, i.e. they are not played too loud.

If a little bit of experimentation puts you off, The Monroe Institute
and CenterPointe both sell pre-packaged tapes and CDs with helpline
support and that kind of thing.

Therefore, use binaural and isochronic sounds consciously and
AT YOUR OWN RISK.

1.1: Isochronic Tones
--------------------

SBaGen+ adds support for isochronic tones, which represent another approach 
to brainwave entrainment that differs significantly from binaural beats.

While binaural beats require two different frequencies played separately to 
each ear to create a beat in the brain, isochronic tones use a single tone 
that is turned on and off at regular intervals (amplitude modulation). This 
creates distinct pulses of sound with clean breaks in between.

The primary advantages of isochronic tones include:

1. They do not require headphones to be effective, as the pulsing occurs
   in the sound itself rather than being created by the brain's processing
   of two different tones.

2. Some research suggests they may produce a stronger entrainment effect
   than binaural beats because the brain responds more strongly to the
   sharp contrast between sound and silence.

3. They can be more effective at higher frequencies (beta and gamma ranges),
   where binaural beats become less perceptible.

In SBaGen+, isochronic tones are implemented by modulating a carrier frequency
with a pulse frequency. For example, a 300Hz carrier tone pulsed at 10Hz
creates an isochronic tone that may help induce an alpha brainwave state.

Both binaural beats and isochronic tones can be effective for different
purposes and individuals. Some people respond better to one method than
the other, so having both options available allows for more personalized
brainwave entrainment experiences.

While headphones are essential for binaural beats, isochronic tones can be 
effective with or without headphones, though headphones may still provide 
a more immersive experience.

Some more theory -- the oscillations in the brain are split into four
'bands' according to frequency:

Delta (0.5 to 4 Hz).  This is normally generated in deep sleep, or
when unconsious.  Those experienced in deep trance states can generate
these waves whilst remaining conscious.

Theta (4 to 8 Hz).  This is the region between sleep and wakefulness,
in which *dreaming* and other dream-like (or 'hypnagogic') experiences
occur.  It's that bit just before you actually fall asleep at night,
or just before you are really awake in the morning.  They say it's in
this band that the unconscious talks to the conscious mind.  It also
seems to be connected with psychic or ESP-type functioning.

Alpha (8 to 13 Hz).  This is produced when you are awake and fully
conscious, but with your awareness focussed inside, such as when
trying to remember something, or when your eyes are closed.

Beta (13 to 30 Hz).  This is normally generated when you are awake,
with the attention focussed outside, dealing with the outside world.
It is also generated when you are solving logical problems, such as
mental arithmetic.

2: Invocation
-----------------------------

The following example commands should be typed at the
shell prompt provided by your operating system, prepared as explained
above.

>> sbagenx

This gives a brief usage information.
 
>> sbagenx -h

This gives the full usage information, showing the version number and
the full list of options.  Check this for any new features.
 
>> sbagenx prog-chakras-1.sbg

This runs the sequence in file 'prog-chakras-1.sbg', starting playing
whatever should be playing at the current moment, according to the
clock time.  The status-line at the bottom of the screen shows the
current time, and currently-playing tones/noise.  The two lines
immediately above show the period of the sequence which is being
played -- these lines scroll up the screen as one period moves into
the next.

Remember that you can stop the utility using Ctrl-C on all platforms:
Windows, macOS and UNIX.  (Most UNIX users already know this, but
it seems that many Windows users don't know about this standard DOS
key combination).

>> sbagenx -p drop 00ds+

This runs one of the built-in pre-defined sequences called "drop" with
the given parameters (see later for an explanation).

>> sbagenx -m river1.ogg -p drop 00ds+ mix/99

This runs the same sequence, but mixes it with water sounds from the
loopable OGG file river1.ogg.

>> sbagenx -m river2.ogg -p slide 200+10/1 mix/99

This runs another built-in pre-defined sequence and mixes it with
other river sounds.  This particular sequence keeps you in alpha for a
half-hour session whilst dropping the carrier right down to zero.

To test a sequence (without waiting the full 24 hours for it to play
through), run with the -q 'quick' option:

>> sbagenx -q 120 prog-chakras-1.sbg

This example runs the sequence at 120 times the normal speed.  This is
quite useful to check that a new sequence is going to do what you
expect it to do.

To check that SBaGenX has interpreted your sequence-file correctly, use
the -D option, which dumps a listing of the interpreted sequence
instead of playing it:

>> sbagenx -D prog-chakras-1.sbg

To quickly test a particular tone-set continuously, use the -i
immediate option.  This is good for experimenting with different
ideas.  If your shell lets you recall the previous command with
up-arrow, you can quickly test and fine-tune a tone-set in this way
before putting it into a sequence:

>> sbagenx -i pink/40 100+1.5/20 200-4/36 400+8/2

This incidentally, is an example of a complex brain-state built from
pink noise, to which is added three frequencies (1.5Hz, 4Hz and 8Hz)
each on an independant carrier (100, 200 and 400 Hz), at different
amplitudes (20, 36 and 2).  This combination is supposedly something
like "body-asleep, brain-awake", assuming I've reproduced it
correctly.

To test isochronic tones, you can use the @ notation. For example:

>> sbagenx -i 300@10/20

This creates an isochronic tone with a carrier frequency of 300Hz,
pulsing at 10Hz (alpha range), with an amplitude of 20%. Unlike
binaural beats, isochronic tones can be effective even without
headphones.

You can optionally customize the isochronic pulse gate using `-I`:

>> sbagenx -I s=0.05:d=0.40 -i 300@10/20

`s` is the cycle start proportion (0 to <1) and `d` is duty (0 to 1].
Additional optional envelope parameters:

  a  attack fraction of the ON window (0..1)
  r  release fraction of the ON window (0..1)
  e  edge-shape mode: 0=hard, 1=linear, 2=smoothstep, 3=smootherstep

Parameters are colon-separated, order-independent, and may be omitted.
Constraint: `a + r <= 1`.
Defaults are:

  s=0.0485, d=0.4030, a=0.5, r=0.5, e=2

These are also the runtime defaults used when `-I` is omitted entirely.
When `-I` is supplied, those custom envelope parameters are now applied
consistently to actual isochronic playback as well as to one-cycle plots.

The defaults above are chosen for legacy compatibility with the original
threshold-gated SBaGen+/SBaGen envelope. If you want a more explicitly
literature-informed starting point for low-frequency envelope salience,
try:

  s=0, d=0.35, a=0.12, r=0.65, e=2

In copy-pasteable `-I` form:

  -I s=0:d=0.35:a=0.12:r=0.65:e=2

This alternative keeps a brisk onset, a longer decay, and substantial
off-time between pulses. It should be treated as an informed experimental
preset rather than a proven optimum. The installer ships comparison plots
in the `Plots` directory, including:

  assets/example-plot-iso-cycle-default.png
  assets/example-plot-iso-cycle-literature-informed.png
  assets/example-plot-iso-cycle-custom00-literal.png

To render a one-cycle isochronic plot (PNG) instead of audio output,
use `-P` with an isochronic (`@`) tone:

>> sbagenx -P -I s=0.05:d=0.40:a=0.50:r=0.50:e=2 -i 200@1/100

This writes a two-panel graph (envelope + waveform) for exactly one
pulse cycle, with labelled numeric ticks and the effective parameters
printed at the bottom.

For isochronic work, `-I` remains the normal recommended control path.
It expresses the pulse shape in explicit structural parameters
(`s/d/a/r/e`) that are easier to reason about, compare, and document
than an arbitrary point list.

For sequence-file work, there is now also a separate `customNN` envelope
definition family intended specifically for intuitive custom shaping where
literal zero really means silence.  Unlike historical `waveNN`, these
samples are treated as a unipolar envelope, scaled only by their maximum,
with optional segment smoothing specified directly on the definition via
`e=<mode>`:

  e=0  hard / step
  e=1  linear (default)
  e=2  smoothstep
  e=3  smootherstep

The `customNN` samples are then interpolated around the cycle with no
global min/max recentering.  Example:

  custom00: e=2 0 0.1 0.9 1 1 0.9 0.1 0
  base: custom00:400@2/20
  00:00 base

`customNN` currently supports binaural tones, isochronic tones, and the
compatible mix-effect envelopes `mixpulse`, `mixspin`, and `mixbeat` on the
`sbagenxlib` path.  For isochronic tones, the custom envelope replaces the
default `-I` pulse shape for that tone.  For mix effects, the custom envelope
modulates effect strength while preserving the underlying sine/explicit
waveform oscillator used by the effect.  The installer ships a rendered
reference plot for the example above as:

For new isochronic designs, `-I` is still the recommended first choice.
Use `customNN` when you specifically want a nonstandard hand-shaped
envelope that cannot be expressed cleanly with the normal `-I`
parameters.

  assets/example-plot-iso-cycle-custom00-literal.png

`-P` plots for these `customNN` isochronic envelopes now work through both
the built-in renderer and the Python/Cairo backend.

If Python+Cairo plotting is available (for example from a bundled
Windows runtime), `-P` uses that high-quality backend automatically.
If not, it falls back to the built-in renderer.

You can also combine isochronic tones with other sounds:

>> sbagenx -i pink/40 300@10/20

This combines pink noise with an isochronic tone in the alpha range.

**Testing Individual Waveforms**

You can also test specific waveforms for individual tones using the
waveform prefix:

>> sbagenx -i square:200+10/20
>> sbagenx -i triangle:300@8/15
>> sbagenx -i sawtooth:150+6/25

Or combine different waveforms in a single test:

>> sbagenx -i sine:200+10/15 square:300@8/10

This creates a complex soundscape with a sine wave binaural beat, a
square wave isochronic tone, and triangle wave modulated pink noise.

To test waveform-modulated spatial effects with background audio:

>> sbagenx -m river1.ogg -i mix/80 triangle:mixspin:400+6.0/40
>> sbagenx -m river1.ogg -i mix/75 square:mixpulse:8/50

These create spatial rotation and pulsing effects with waveform modulation.

While you're at it, try the alternate 'spin' effect.  Not everyone
likes this, but in any case it's there if you want to use it:

>> sbagenx -i spin:300+4.2/80

You can also try the 'mixspin' effect, which applies a spatial rotation to
audio input instead of to pink noise:

>> sbagenx -m river1.ogg -i mix/80 mixspin:500+8.0/50

The mixspin effect creates an immersive spatial experience where the sound
appears to rotate around your head. The width parameter (500μs in this example)
controls how wide the rotation effect is, the frequency (8.0Hz) controls how
fast it rotates, and the intensity (50%) controls the strength of the effect.

You can also try the 'mixpulse' effect, which applies a pulse effect to the
audio input instead of to pink noise:

>> sbagenx -m river1.ogg -i mix/80 mixpulse:10/50

The mixpulse effect creates a rhythmic pulse effect where the sound intensity
fluctuates at the given rate. The pulse rate is 10Hz and the intensity is 50%.

You can also try the 'mixbeat' effect, which applies opposite frequency shifts
to left/right channels (Hilbert method) to embed a beat into the mix stream:

>> sbagenx -m river1.ogg -i mix/80 mixbeat:6/45

In this example, the embedded beat target is 6Hz and the effect intensity is
45%. The cleanest result is usually with the default sine waveform.

If you're having trouble with your soundcard or CPU usage on a very
old machine, use the -r or -b options to run at a lower output rate or
bit-width.  For example:

>> sbagenx -r 8000 -i pink/40 100+1.5/20 200+6/30
>> sbagenx -b 8 -i pink/40 100+1.5/20 200+6/30

SBaGenX includes several additional options for enhanced control:

The -V option allows you to set a global volume level from 1 to 100 
(default is 100). This affects the final output volume without changing 
the relative amplitudes between different tones in your sequence:

>> sbagenx -V 50 -i 200+10/20
>> sbagenx -V 25 -m river1.ogg -p drop 00ds+ mix/99

The -w option allows you to select the waveform type for binaural beats
and isochronic tones. Available options are: sine (default), square,
triangle, and sawtooth. Note that sine waves are generally recommended
for binaural beats and isochronic tones as they produce the smoothest
entrainment effect:

>> sbagenx -w sine -i 200+10/20      # Default sine wave
>> sbagenx -w square -i 200+10/20    # Square wave (more aggressive)
>> sbagenx -w triangle -i 200+10/20  # Triangle wave (moderate)
>> sbagenx -w sawtooth -i 200+10/20  # Sawtooth wave

The -N option disables automatic amplitude normalization. By default, 
SBaGenX automatically normalizes amplitudes when the total exceeds 100% 
to prevent audio distortion. Using -N allows the original behavior where 
amplitude totals exceeding 100% may cause clipping and distortion:

>> sbagenx -N -i pink/60 200+10/50   # May cause distortion if total > 100%
>> sbagenx -i pink/60 200+10/50      # Automatically normalized (default)


For UNIX and macOS, you'll find that there are a set of short
tone-set scripts (t-*) that you can run from the command-line.  For
example:

>> ls t-*
>> t-alpha

All these executable tone-set scripts (t-*) accept additional options
and pass them onto SBaGen, so you can use the same options (e.g. -r
and -b) with these as well.

Try out some of these scripts/batch files to get an idea of some of
the possibilities.


For all platforms (UNIX, Windows and macOS), the sequence files
(prog-*) can be used in the same way:

>> sbagenx prog-NSDWS-example.sbg

Have a play with these sequences.  Some of them are designed to run at
night, so you will hear nothing if you play them during the day.  You
get around this by playing them immediately using '-q 1', or '-S' (run
from start):

>> sbagenx -q 1 prog-NSDWS-example.sbg
>> sbagenx -S prog-NSDWS-example.sbg

On UNIX and macOS, there are also a few Perl-scripts which you can
run (p-*).  These generate a sequence on the fly and then run it.  For
anyone familiar with Perl, this is a good way to generate sequences
that are slightly different each night in a random way.  For example:

>> p-nightly-sub-delta 7:30

This generates a night-time sequence in 'tmp-prog' with a wake-up time
of 7:30 and runs it.  Naturally you won't hear anything unless it is
already night time.


2.1: Outputting to a pipe or a file
-----------------------------------

You don't need to read this section if you are only interested in
playing through your soundcard.

If you wish to pipe output on to some other utility, use the -O
option.  When this is used, the real time is no longer checked, so you
can output to both real-time devices (such as a mixer daemon), or to a
file or effect utility.  It is assumed that the eventual output device
will use exactly the sampling frequency specified, or otherwise all
the frequencies and timings will be out.

For raw/WAV output, `-b` now accepts `24` as well as `8` and `16`.
The normal default remains `16`, except that `.flac` output on the
`sbagenxlib`-backed runtime path uses 24-bit automatically.
The 24-bit path currently applies to the `sbagenxlib`-backed runtime
engine (for example `-i`, built-in `-p` programs, and the modern
library-backed sequence paths), and writes 24-bit little-endian PCM for
raw output and 24-bit PCM inside WAV files.

The -o option can also be used to output directly to a file.  By
default this writes headerless raw data (to write a WAV file, see the
-W option below).  If the filename given to -o ends with .mp3, .ogg or
.flac, SBaGenX will encode directly to that format (using runtime
encoder libraries).  The -O option remains raw PCM only.  For example,
to pipe to bplay under UNIX, you could use the following:

Windows packaging note: when installed via the Windows installer, the
correct architecture runtime DLLs are promoted to canonical names
(e.g. libsndfile-1.dll, libmp3lame-0.dll) automatically.  If you run
dist/sbagenx-win32.exe or dist/sbagenx-win64.exe directly (without the
installer), that promotion step is not run.

>> sbagenx -r 8000 -O -i 100+0.92/30 200+4.2/15 | bplay -S -s 8000 -b 16

To output an isochronic tone to a file:

>> sbagenx -r 44100 -o isochronic-alpha.raw -i 300@10/20

To encode directly:

>> sbagenx -r 44100 -o session.flac -L 0:20 -i 200+8/30
>> sbagenx -r 44100 -o session.ogg  -L 0:20 -i 200+8/30
>> sbagenx -r 44100 -o session.mp3  -L 0:20 -i 200+8/30

Encoding tuning options:

  -K <kbps>   MP3 bitrate in kbps (8..320, default 192)
  -J <q>      MP3 quality (0..9, lower is better, default 2)
  -X <q>      MP3 VBR quality (0..9, lower is better; enables VBR mode)
  -U <q>      OGG Vorbis quality (0..10; default 6)
  -Z <level>  FLAC compression level (0..12; lossless in all cases)

If -X is provided, MP3 variable bitrate mode is used and -K is ignored.

When the runtime is handled by `sbagenxlib` (which now covers built-in
programs and the modern sequence-backed paths), `.flac` output is fed
to libsndfile through a 24-bit PCM path instead of being truncated to
16-bit first. On that same library-backed runtime path, `.ogg` output is
handed to libsndfile as normalized float samples instead of being
quantized to 16-bit first. MP3 now follows the same float handoff path
when the available LAME runtime exposes its float-input API; otherwise
it falls back to the existing 16-bit handoff.

Examples:

>> sbagenx -r 44100 -o session.mp3 -K 224 -J 2 -L 0:20 -i 200+8/30
>> sbagenx -r 44100 -o session.mp3 -X 2.5 -J 2 -L 0:20 -i 200+8/30
>> sbagenx -r 44100 -o session.ogg -U 6 -L 0:20 -i 200+8/30
>> sbagenx -r 44100 -o session.flac -Z 8 -L 0:20 -i 200+8/30
>> sbagenx -r 44100 -b 24 -o session.raw -L 0:20 -i 200+8/30

The -L option allows a time-limit to be set on the output.  So, for
example, to output 45 minutes of data to a file, and then play it with
bplay, use:

>> sbagenx -r 8000 -o out.raw -L 0:45 -i 100+0.92/30 200+4.2/15
>> bplay -S -s 8000 -b 16 out.raw

As an alternative to using -L, it is possible to use -S and -E to
indicate that the output starts at the start of the sequence file
rather than the real time (-S), and/or ends at the end of the sequence
file rather than going on forever (-E).  Together (-SE) the sequence
runs from start to end.  It is also possible to start at a particular
clock time using the -T option.

A WAV file can be output using the -W option if one of the above
options (-L or -SE) is also used to fix the length.  The -W option
should be used in conjunction with -o or -O.  So, repeating the
examples above using WAV files, we have:

>> sbagenx -r 8000 -WO -L 1:00 -i 100+0.92/30 200+4.2/15 | bplay

and:

>> sbagenx -r 8000 -Wo out.wav -L 0:45 -i 100+0.92/30 200+4.2/15
>> bplay out.wav

For 24-bit WAV output on the `sbagenxlib` runtime path:

>> sbagenx -r 44100 -b 24 -Wo out24.wav -L 0:20 -i 200+8/30

The -Q option may be useful to turn off the information that the
utility normally writes to the screen.

If you are using another utility (perl or whatever) to generate the
sequence-file, you can pipe it directly to the utility, and use '-' as
the filename.  For example:

>> cat prog-chakras-1.sbg | sbagenx -

For those pushing the utility past its design limits, you may wish to
use the following options: 

'-R rate' specifies the rate at which the sound parameters are
recalculated in Hz, the default being 10Hz.  10Hz is fine for a slide
that takes a minute, but if you are doing quick (1 sec) fades in and
out, you'll need a faster refresh to avoid clicks.

'-F fade_interval' specifies the time in ms over which a fade in or
out takes place.  This is normally one minute (60000ms), which is fine
for slowly changing tone-sets, but you may wish to adjust this value
according to your needs. In current SBaGenX, explicit '-F' also affects
the fade-out tail used by the native built-in '-p' programs.


2.2: Mixing in background sounds
--------------------------------

There are two options to allow you to mix in a background soundtrack
with the binaurals or isochronic as they are generated.  This is
generally regarded as a good idea, and this technique is used by both
CenterPointe and the Monroe Institute. The binaural beats/isochronic
tones are generated at a low level (e.g. 200+4/1.0), so that they are
only just audible when mixed with the louder soundtrack (of water sounds
or whatever).

The soundtrack should be either in the form of an MP3 file, an OGG
file, a 16-bit stereo WAV file, or raw 16-bit little-endian stereo
audio file.  By specifying the -m option, the given file is mixed with
the generated binaural beats:

>> sbagenx -m background.wav -i 200+4/1

or
 
>> sbagenx -m river1.ogg -i 200+4/1

When the soundtrack file ends, sbagen will stop playing.  For loopable
OGGs, though (see later), the soundtrack goes on forever.  By default
sbagen will change its sample rate to match the given WAV, MP3 or OGG
file, unless you override it with a -r option.  (Note that in the Mac
OS X version of SBaGen 1.2.0, I still haven't added the CoreAudio
sample-rate conversion, so you always end up with the default output
rate at the moment; let me know if this needs fixing urgently).

Note that if the soundtrack is too loud, then the addition of the
binaural beats might cause clipping.  However, you can reduce the
amplitude of the soundtrack using "mix" to fit within the maximum
total limit of 100.  For example:

>> sbagenx -m soundtrack.mp3 -i mix/99 200+4/1

"Mix" entries may also be used in sequence files to fade the
soundtrack in and out.

The -M option allows raw sound data to be passed on the standard
input.  This is useful in combination with other tools that generate
audio.  For example, you can use 'splay' on UNIX as an external MP3
player instead of using the built-in one, for example:

>> splay -d - background.mp3 | sbagenx -M -i mix/99 200+4/1

Piping data in like this has the advantage of not requiring a huge WAV
file to be stored on your disk.


2.3: Standard background sounds
-------------------------------

Original SBaGen comes with two standard OGG files that loop themselves
endlessly in a random way that provides an endless stream of river
sounds with no discernable pattern.  These are used with the -m
option.  For example:

  sbagenx -m river1.ogg -i 200+10/1
  sbagenx -m river1.ogg#1 -i 200+10/1
  sbagenx -m river2.ogg -i 200+10/1
  sbagenx -m river2.ogg#1 -i 200+10/1

The '#1' part allows you to select different looping setups from the

volume level, for example:

  sbagenx -m river1.ogg -i 200+10/1 mix/50

2.4: Creating loopable OGG/MP3/FLAC mixes
-----------------------------------------

This section will be of interest only to those people who want to
create special loopable OGG, MP3 or FLAC files to use with SBaGen, like the
provided river1.ogg and river2.ogg files.

The cross-fading and looping settings for any particular OGG/MP3/FLAC file are
controlled by a spec-string embedded in the file with the tag
'SBAGEN_LOOPER'.  This may be set using the standard OGG
'vorbiscomment' tool, for example:

  vorbiscomment -a in.ogg out.ogg -t "SBAGEN_LOOPER=s4-16 f0.2" 

For native FLAC files, set the same tag with:

  metaflac --set-tag="SBAGEN_LOOPER=s4-16 f0.2" in.flac

For MP3 files, store the same value in an ID3v2 TXXX frame with
description SBAGEN_LOOPER.  For example with ffmpeg:

  ffmpeg -i in.wav -c:a libmp3lame -id3v2_version 3 \
    -metadata "TXXX:SBAGEN_LOOPER=s4-16 f0.2" out.mp3

The embedded tag remains supported, but SBaGenX can now also override
it explicitly from the command line or GUI.  On the command line, use:

  --looper "i s928 f5 c1 w1 d218-1146"

Double quotes are recommended because the spec contains spaces and they
work on both Linux shells and Windows command prompts.  An explicit
override takes precedence over any embedded SBAGEN_LOOPER tag.

The tag contains entries as follows, optionally separated by white
space:

  i             If this is the first char after SBAGEN_LOOPER= and is
                followed by whitespace, play the
                section from start-of-file up to d<min> once as an intro
  s<dur>        Set segment size to given duration in seconds
  s<min>-<max>  Set segment size to randomly vary within given range
  f<dur>        Set duration for cross-fades
  c<cnt>        Number of channels: 1 or 2
  w<bool>       Swap stereo on second channel? 0 no, or 1 yes
  d<min>-<max>  Select section of OGG/MP3/FLAC file data to use as source audio
  #<digits>     Following settings apply only to one section
  
The optional intro mode ('i') only has an effect when d<min> is greater
than zero.  When enabled, the first post-intro segment always starts
at d<min> and begins immediately (without a fade-in) for a seamless
intro-to-loop transition.

The default is something like "s99999999 f1 c1 w1 d0-99999999",
i.e. segments are the full length of the audio, taken out of the whole
of the audio file, repeated forever with a 1 second cross-fade at
start/end.

Note that with 'c1', only one audio segment is playing at any one time
(except for those moments when there is a cross-fade taking place from
one segment to the next).  However, with 'c2', two segments will be
playing constantly.  This means that if 'c1' sounds like a small
river, 'c2' will sound like a big river!  By default the second
playback channel has left and right swapped over, so that the sound is
always balanced; 'w0' makes it unswapped.

The scheduling required to select random segments of audio that won't
interfere with one another or repeat anything already heard recently
is quite complex, but it should all work without problem so long as
the maximum segment size is no more than around 25% of the total
length of the source file.  If the maximum segment size is longer, then
everything will still work, but there is a very small chance that you
may get 'echo' effects at times (e.g. when the same segment is played
on both channels at nearly the same time).

The # part of the spec-string works as follows.  The .ogg/.flac filename on
the command line may be followed by #<digits>.  If this is missing, it
is equivalent to #0.  This allows different groups of settings to be
selected out of the SBAGEN_LOOPER string.  As an example (spaced to
make it more readable):

  SBAGEN_LOOPER=s4-16  #0 f0.2  #1 f0.5  #2 f1

The initial part is read in all cases, and then #0, #1 and #2 can be
used to select different cross-fade times.


2.4.1: Using ReplayGain with OGG/FLAC files
-------------------------------------------

SBaGenX supports ReplayGain tags in OGG/FLAC metadata.  What this means
is that if you run 'vorbisgain' on your OGG file, or set the
REPLAYGAIN_TRACK_GAIN tag in FLAC metadata, your file should come out
sounding around about the same loudness level as everyone else's files
(including river1.ogg and river2.ogg).  SBaGen picks this up and
adjusts the volume level accordingly.  This makes it easier for people
to substitute one soundtrack for another in their sequences.  (Thanks
to Dylan Carlson for
suggesting this.)


2.5: Compensating for headphone low frequency roll-off
------------------------------------------------------

For those who are interested in working with especially low carrier
frequencies, please read this section.  Everyone else can skip it --
you don't need to use this for running most sequences.  It only
becomes useful for later -p drop sessions, or if you want to enhance
the effect of -p slide.

Your headphones and audio equipment will naturally respond less at
lower frequencies, and how much the amplitude 'rolls off' depends on
many things.  However, SBaGen can compensate for this by increasing
the amplitude of the sine waves it is generating.  All you have to do
is give it a set of frequency/amplitude points, and it does the rest.
The compensation is quite intelligent.  It works on the individual
output frequencies on left and right channels, so if the left channel
has a lower frequency, it will be boosted more.  Also, if increasing
the beat amplitudes would cause other sound sources (such as the mix
stream) to overload the outputs, those sources are reduced in
amplitude.

To set this up, you have to decide how much you want the lower
frequencies boosted by.  If you know something about electronics, you
may be able to measure this, but if not you could try a few
frequencies and adjust the amplitude by ear until they sound okay.
For example, you could try the following:

  sbagenx -i 160/1
  sbagenx -i 80/1
  sbagenx -i 40/1
  sbagenx -i 30/1
  sbagenx -i 20/1

Now try adjusting the amplitudes of the lower tones to make them
audible in some way.  Don't overdo it, though -- remember your ears
are also becoming less sensitive as the frequencies go down, and you
are only trying to make the tones seem present, not necessary as loud.
Also, you might damage equipment if you boost sub-bass frequencies too
far (watch your speaker cones!).  Once you have a set of specs that
you are happy with (let's say 80/1, 40/2, 30/4 and 20/6), you can put
them into a -c specification as follows:
  
  -c 80=1,40=2,30=4,20=6

Note that the highest frequency should always have an adjust of 1,
because this is what will be used for all the frequencies above that.

If you add this to the sbagen command-line, then all sine-waves output
will be adjusted according to this.  All tones with frequencies above
the highest frequency in the list are multiplied by the factor on the
highest, and all below the lowest frequency are multipled by the
factor on the lowest.  The frequencies in between use a factor
established by linear interpolation, so in this case 60Hz gets a
factor of 1.5 from being half-way between 80Hz and 40Hz.  You are not
limited to the frequencies above (80,40,30,20), incidentally -- you
can use any frequencies you like.

In this way, you should be able to make better use of your existing
equipment to reach the lower frequencies.  This is especially
interesting for the later levels of -p drop and also for all the -p
slide sequences.  However, do remember that your -c option applies
only to your own particular soundcard, amplifier and headphones, so
please remove or comment out any -c options in any sequences you send
to other people.


2.6: The 'drop' sequences
-------------------------

This is a series of sequences that are designed to be used fairly
often and over a long period of time (e.g. daily over several years).
By working through all these sequences, you stimulate all the
different combinations of carrier frequency and beat frequency over a
large range (0-200Hz carrier, 0.3-10Hz beats).  According to some
people's experiences, doing this releases mental energy for reuse.
You have to work through this over a long period, though, because the
stirred up energy sometimes needs time to be reprocessed by your body.

(For those familiar with Toltec traditions, this is a form of
Recapitulation.  For those familiar with Chi and Taoist practices, you
could say that this stirs up heavy chi for reprocessing and reuse.
Those who have used regression may experience the processing of
stirred up energy as reliving and releasing of past painful or
emotional events.  There are many approaches and interpretations that
can be applied to the same thing.)

For guidelines on how to use this method, start at a particular level,
e.g. level 00, and listen to that level until you get 'bored' of it,
i.e. until it seems to be having no more effect.  Then move onto level
01 and do the same.  If you want to move ahead more quickly, you can
step two levels at a time (there is some overlap between levels), but
working one level at a time is recommended.

There are two main things you may experience at each level:

- A boost of energy, which is generally regarded as a good thing.

- An apparent drop in energy, due to your body working to reprocess
  the old stirred up energy.  If this is mild, keep using the same
  level until it clears, at which point you may get a boost.  However,
  if it seems too much, then stop listening for a while until you feel
  stronger again, and come back to it.  Any other activities that you
  do that enhance your physical or energetic well-being and health
  will help you with working through any particularly difficult
  levels.

If you work too fast through the levels (e.g. listening to too many
different levels in a short space of time without finishing any of
them), you may reach a state of 'overload' or 'overstimulation', with
too much going on in your head or other problems like that.  In this
case, take a rest from the sequences and wait for things to get back
to normal.  Then you can approach them again a little more carefully.

The levels are labelled with a numeric level and a letter indicating
how deep the sequence takes you.  Non-negative levels are usually
written with two digits (00 to 99).  Negative levels are written with
a leading minus sign (for example -01).  For example, 00d is a good
starting point.  To this you add "s+" to indicate sliding and an
hour-long sequence.  It is best to also mix these with river sounds,
for example:

  sbagenx -m river1.ogg -p drop 00ds+ mix/100
  sbagenx -m river1.ogg -p drop 00ds+M mix/100

If you are doing sessions in the middle of the day, you may want to
add a 'wake-up' at the end using '^', which brings you up to alpha
again at the end:

  sbagenx -m river1.ogg -p drop 00ds+^ mix/100

If you want to use an isochronic instead of a binaural tone, you can
use '@' on the end of the level specification:

  sbagenx -m river1.ogg -p drop 00ds+@/3 white/10

To render the drop beat/pulse curve as an image instead of running
audio output, add `-G` with `-p drop`:

  sbagenx -G -p drop t40,10,5 -01ds+
  sbagenx --graph-video drop.mp4 --graph-video-fps 30 \
          -G -p drop t40,10,5 -01ds+

This writes a PNG graph of the beat/pulse curve over the drop period
only, then exits.  The graph uses whole-number tick labels on both
axes (time in minutes and frequency in Hz).  `@` and `M` are reflected
in the plotted mode label (pulse vs monaural beat).

If `--graph-video file.mp4` is supplied as well, SBaGenX also writes an
MP4 animation of the same graph with the plotted-period audio muxed
into the video.  The video uses a tracking cursor and a pulsating dot
whose motion follows the plotted beat/pulse curve over the drop period.

Compatibility fallback: `-P` also triggers this program graph when no
cycle-plot target is present (`-H` absent and no `mixam:<...>` extra).

Once you have completed level 00, you can move onto the next (01, 02,
03, etc).  However if you like, instead you can try increasing the
depth of the session.  In 00ds+, the 'd' indicates that it goes down
to 2.5Hz (a delta frequency).  The lightest is 'a' (e.g. 00as+), which
only goes down to 4.4Hz (theta), whilst the deepest is 'l', which goes
right down to 0.3Hz (sub-delta).  The aim is to go as far as you can
with both the levels and the depth (a-l).  You have to judge
your progress for yourself, though -- trying very deep levels to start
with won't work, because you won't be able to entrain that low without
lots of practice.


2.6.1: More detailed notes on -p drop options
---------------------------------------------

Usage: 

  -p drop
      <signed-level|N><target-spec>[s|k][+][^][@|M][/<amp>][:S=<val>]
      <tone-specs> ...
  -G -p drop [t<drop>,<hold>,<wake>]
      <signed-level|N><target-spec>[s|k][+][^][@|M][/<amp>][:S=<val>]

Examples:

  -p drop 00a
  -p drop 00ds+
  -p drop 20fs+^/2 mix/98
  -p drop 34.5dk+/1.5 pink/30
  -p drop -01ds+ white/10
  -p drop 00d@/3 brown/15
  -p drop 00d+M/2
  -p drop 00ls+:S=2 mix/100 mixam:beat
  -p drop Nls+:S=2 mix/100 mixam:beat
  -A -p drop 00ds+/3 mix/97
  -A d=0.3:e=0.3:k=10:E=0.7 -p drop 00ds+/3 mix/97

The argument after the "drop" gives the level and depth of the
sequence to run.  `<signed-level>` may be non-negative (00, 01, ...,
99) or negative (-01, -02, ...), and it may include a decimal point
for fine tuning (for example 20.5a).
Using `N` in place of the signed level mutes generated program tones
while still generating the beat/pulse trajectory (for example to drive
`mixam:beat` without adding a separate binaural/isochronic/monaural tone).

Carrier formula (for quick reference):
  Let L = signed level value
  Starting carrier (Hz):   f_start = 205 - 2*L
  Reverse lookup:          L = (205 - f_start) / 2

Examples:
  L=00  -> f_start=205Hz
  L=20  -> f_start=165Hz
  L=-01 -> f_start=207Hz

Negative levels are important if you want carriers above the 00
baseline, because many binaural-beat study protocols use carriers
around 200Hz and above.  Values such as -01, -02, ... make this range
easy to target while keeping the same drop-session behavior.

During the drop phase, the carrier still falls by 5Hz at t=D.  If '+'
is used, the carrier keeps dropping during hold at the same linear rate
as in the drop phase.  So the extra hold drop is:

  5 * (hold-time / drop-time) Hz

(before any wake-up '^' segment).  The idea is usually to work upward
through levels, waiting for each to become 'boring' before moving on.
(In theory, the whole set could take several years to work through.)
Later deep levels require headphones that can reproduce really low
bass frequencies, ideally down to 10Hz or lower.

The target spec selects the beat/pulse endpoint.  Beat/pulse frequencies
default to a 10Hz start, then drop exponentially to the selected target.
You can override the start value with `:S=<val>` in Hz.

Target-spec forms:
  a ... l         base targets from the table below
  +a ... +l       same as the positive form above
  a.x ... l.x     interpolate fractionally toward the next deeper letter
  -a ... -l       mirror the positive scale upward around `a = 4.4Hz`
  -a.x ... -l.x   same mirrored rule, with fractional interpolation

Examples:
  a    = 4.4Hz
  a.5  = midpoint between a and b = 4.05Hz
  l.5  = midpoint between l=0.3 and synthetic next target 0.2 = 0.25Hz
  -a   = 4.4Hz
  -b   = 5.1Hz

The base letter targets are: 4.4, 3.7, 3.1, 2.5, 2.0, 1.5, 1.2, 0.9,
0.7, 0.5, 0.4, 0.3.  Using `a`, you never get down to delta frequencies.
The deeper beats (i.e. later letters) are supposed to be better, so long
as you really are entraining to them.  Like the carriers, this is
something to build up over time.

Reference (lowest beat/pulse target by letter):
  +--------+-----+-----+-----+-----+-----+-----+
  | Letter |  a  |  b  |  c  |  d  |  e  |  f  |
  +--------+-----+-----+-----+-----+-----+-----+
  | Hz     | 4.4 | 3.7 | 3.1 | 2.5 | 2.0 | 1.5 |
  +--------+-----+-----+-----+-----+-----+-----+
  | Letter |  g  |  h  |  i  |  j  |  k  |  l  |
  +--------+-----+-----+-----+-----+-----+-----+
  | Hz     | 1.2 | 0.9 | 0.7 | 0.5 | 0.4 | 0.3 |
  +--------+-----+-----+-----+-----+-----+-----+

Reference (mirrored negative target by letter):
  +--------+------+------+------+------+------+------+
  | Letter | -a   | -b   | -c   | -d   | -e   | -f   |
  +--------+------+------+------+------+------+------+
  | Hz     | 4.4  | 5.1  | 5.7  | 6.3  | 6.8  | 7.3  |
  +--------+------+------+------+------+------+------+
  | Letter | -g   | -h   | -i   | -j   | -k   | -l   |
  +--------+------+------+------+------+------+------+
  | Hz     | 7.6  | 7.9  | 8.1  | 8.3  | 8.4  | 8.5  |
  +--------+------+------+------+------+------+------+

You have three options regarding how the frequencies change during the
session.  By default (00d+) the frequencies go down in steps, changing
every 3 minutes.  With 'k' added (00dk+) the frequencies go down in
shorter 1 minute steps.  With 's' added (00ds+) there are no steps,
and the frequencies smoothly and gradually change throughout the whole
session, which gives less experience of specifics.  In SBaGenX, this
sliding mode is evaluated from a runtime function-driven curve for
carrier and beat/pulse frequencies rather than from coarse piecewise
interpolation.  The two stepping
options are interesting because if you get into a dreaming state you
may notice that the dream-scene changes for every step.  It is as if
that scene is somehow attached to those frequencies, and by being held
on the frequencies you go deeper into it.  With a sliding session
(00ds+), maybe you never stay long enough on any one frequency to find
the scene, but instead you are gently kept moving though the
frequencies without stopping.  I have been using the sliding option
for my sessions, but some people may like to experiment with the
stepping options.

The basic 'drop' through the beat frequencies occurs in the first 30
minutes of the session.  By adding a '+' (00ds versus 00ds+) an extra
30 minutes are added keeping you at the target beat frequency whilst
still continuing to gently drop the carrier.

Normally the sequence fades out at the deepest beat frequency.
However, if you need to get on with your day immediately after doing a
session, you may want to add the 'wake-up' option by adding a '^'.
This adds a 3-minute slide back up to the initial frequencies to wake
you up.

The optional '/<amp>' on the end lets you change the amplitude of the
waves if necessary (e.g. 00ds+/2).  By default, the level is set to 1.

Adding `@` switches `-p drop` from binaural beat mode to isochronic
pulse mode.  In that case, the same target-spec and timing rules apply
to pulse frequency instead of binaural beat frequency.

Example:

  -p drop 00ds+@/3

Adding `M` in the same modifier position selects monaural mode directly
in the drop spec.  In monaural mode, `-p drop` uses two tones per step:

  f1 = carrier - beat/2
  f2 = carrier + beat/2

Each ear receives both tones (instead of the usual left/right split
used for binaural beats).  `@` and monaural mode are mutually exclusive.
The same target-spec and timing rules apply to monaural frequency
instead of binaural beat frequency.

Mix modulation parameters (via `-A`):

Rationale (conjectural): the linear decay term `V(t)` provides gradual
quietening that may feel soothing, while the periodic dip term `G(t)`
can intermittently heighten awareness.
Reference graph: assets/example-mix-amplitude-modulation.png
(generated for t30,30,0 with defaults d=0.3, e=0.3, k=10, E=0.7).

With `-A`, the mix amplitude is multiplied by:

  G(t) = 1 - d * exp( -e * ((t mod 2k) - k)^2 )
  V(t) = 1 - ((1 - E) / T) * t
  F(t) = G(t) * V(t)

Note: period/tone-set displays still show the base `mix/<amp>` value.
When `-A` is active, the runtime status line shows `mix/<value>` as the
effective mix amplitude after modulation.

Where:
  t = minutes from start of session (excluding wake phase)
  T = total main session length in minutes (drop + hold if `+` is used)
  d, e, k, E are user parameters

Defaults (when `-A` is used without a spec):
  d=0.3, e=0.3, k=10, E=0.7

Optional overrides are provided via `-A` as colon-separated tokens:

  -A d=<val>:e=<val>:k=<val>:E=<val>

If `^` wake-up is present, wake mix amplitude uses:

  W(t) = E + ((1 - E) / U) * t

Where `t` is minutes from wake start, and `U` is wake length in
minutes.

If there is no wake-up phase, the modulation holds at the end level
`E` once the main session has finished instead of jumping back to full
mix level.

After the spec comes an optional list of normal tone-sets.  These can
be used to add mix sounds (e.g. mix/100) or pink noise (pink/30), or
brown noise (brown/15), or white noise (white/10), or other sounds to
the beats.  Anything you add here will automatically be faded in and
out at the start and end of the session.


2.6.2: Adjusting the length of a -p drop session
------------------------------------------------

An optional specification may be added after -p drop and before the
drop specification to adjust the length of the -p drop session from
the standard of a 30-minute 'drop' part, optional 30-minute hold and
optional 3-minute wake-up.  This is formatted as follows:

  t<drop-time>,<hold-time>,<wake-time>

For example:
 
  t40,10,5

The above example gives a 40-minute drop part, optional 10 minute
hold, and optional 5 minute wake-up.  It might be used as follows:

  sbagen -m river2.ogg -p drop t40,10,5 20ds+^/2 mix/98

This way you can create -p drop sessions of whatever length you wish.
Built-in `-p` programs are finite by default and stop automatically at
their programmed end time.  You only need `-L` if you want to force a
different output length.

Also note that lengths are accepted in whole minutes only, and that
you must include the lengths for all three parts even if you are not
using `+` or `^` for a hold or wake-up part.  In stepped (`k`) mode,
the internal checkpoints still follow the normal 1-minute or 3-minute
grid, but the overall programmed runtime is not extended to match that
grid.


2.7: The 'slide' sequences
--------------------------

Usage:

  -p slide [t<duration>] <carr><sign><beat>/<amp> <tone-specs> ...
  where <sign> is +, -, @ (isochronic), or M (monaural)

Example:

  -p slide 200+10/1
  -p slide t60 200+10/1 mix/100
  -p slide 200@10/1
  -p slide 200M10/1

This provides a series of sequences that hold the beat frequency
constant whilst dropping the carrier tone from the initial value right
down to 0Hz.  This is another way of covering all the combinations of
carrier frequency and beat frequency (as -p drop does), but working in
the other direction.  These are also interesting because -- let's say
we use the example of "-p slide 200+10/1" -- in this sequence you are
held at the alpha frequency of 10Hz for the whole session.  People
have reported feeling 'bright and awake' after this kind of session,
because it covers a lot of frequencies, but it doesn't take them too
deep.

If you want a deeper session, though, you could try 200+8/1 or
200+6/1.  You could also try working through the beat frequencies in
0.1 or 0.2Hz steps if you want to try this as an alternative to -p
drop for working through all the combinations.

The sessions are 30 minutes long by default.  If you want a longer
session, you can add 't<duration>' after the 'slide', for example "-p
slide t60 200+6/1" for a 60 minute session.

Built-in `-p` programs are finite by default and stop automatically at
their programmed end time.  You only need `-L` if you want to force a
different output length.

With sign `M`, `-p slide` uses:

  f1 = carrier - beat/2
  f2 = carrier + beat/2

Each ear receives both tones.  `@` and monaural mode are mutually
exclusive.
The same beat and timing rules apply to monaural frequency
instead of binaural beat frequency.

Another point is that the lower frequencies will probably not be
audible on your headphones.  This is not a big problem, but if you
want to try and get a little more out of your equipment, you could
experiment with the -c amplitude-compensation option (see the section
above).


2.8: Buffer Size Configuration for Bluetooth Devices on macOS
------------------------------------------------------------

Usage:

  -B size

Example:

  sbagenx -B 2048 -i 200+10/1
  
  or in a sequence file:
  
  -B 2048 # optional comment

On macOS, some users may experience audio distortion when using
Bluetooth devices. This occurs because the default audio buffer size may
not be suitable for the higher latency of these wireless devices.

The -B option allows you to set a custom buffer size (in samples) for
audio output on macOS. Larger values generally result in fewer
distortions on Bluetooth devices, but may slightly increase latency.

Important points about this option:

1. The value must be a power of 2 (e.g., 1024, 2048, 4096, 8192)
2. The value must be between 1024 and the 8192
3. This option is only available for macOS users
4. Recommended values for Bluetooth devices: 2048 or 4096

If you're using Bluetooth headphones or wireless speakers and notice
distortions, crackling, or interruptions in the audio, try adding the
-B option with a larger value. For example:

  sbagenx -m river1.ogg -B 2048 -p drop 00ds+ mix/99

This option can also be included directly in sequence files, as shown in
the example above. This is useful for creating sequences optimized for
Bluetooth devices without having to specify the option on the command
line.


2.9: Sample Rate Considerations
------------------------------

Usage:

  -r rate

Example:

  sbagenx -r 44100 -i 200+10/1

SBaGenX was designed and optimized for a sample rate of 44.1kHz. While
the program supports different sample rates through the -r option, using
44.1kHz is recommended for the following reasons:

1. Timing Accuracy: The internal calculations and timing of sequences
   are based on 44.1kHz. Using a different sample rate may result in
   timing discrepancies, where sequences may play faster or slower than
   intended.

2. Audio Quality: The binaural beat frequencies (typically 0.5-30Hz)
   and carrier frequencies (50-1500Hz) are well within the Nyquist
   frequency limit of 44.1kHz (22.05kHz), ensuring accurate
   reproduction.

3. Performance: Higher sample rates (48kHz, 96kHz, etc.) will increase
   CPU usage and memory consumption without providing any benefit for
   binaural beats or isochronic tones.

4. Compatibility: 44.1kHz is the standard sample rate for most audio
   devices and ensures the best compatibility across different systems.

If you need to use a different sample rate (e.g., for compatibility with
specific hardware), be aware that:
- Sequence timing may be affected
- CPU usage may increase with higher rates
- The -r option is primarily useful for audio file export or when
  specific hardware requires a different rate

For best results and accurate timing, it is recommended to use the
default 44.1kHz sample rate.


2.10: The 'sigmoid' sequences
-----------------------------

The `-p sigmoid` sequences are similar to `-p drop`, but with an
important difference in how beat/pulse frequencies are shaped in
sliding mode.  In `...s...` mode, the beat/pulse follows a sigmoid
curve over the drop interval.  This allows a gentler entry and exit,
with more change through the middle of the drop period.

Like `-p drop`, these sessions use a level/depth code.  For example:

  sbagenx -m river1.ogg -p sigmoid 00ds+:l=0.125:h=0 mix/100
  sbagenx -m river1.ogg -p sigmoid 00ds+M:l=0.125:h=0 mix/100

You can also use isochronic pulse mode by adding `@`:

  sbagenx -m river1.ogg -p sigmoid 00ds+@/3:l=0.125:h=0 white/10

And you can use signed levels, including negative values, exactly as
with `-p drop`:

  sbagenx -p sigmoid -01ds+:l=0.125:h=0
  sbagenx -p sigmoid 00ls+:l=0.125:h=0:S=2 mix/100 mixam:beat

To render the sigmoid beat/pulse curve as an image instead of running
audio output, add `-G` with `-p sigmoid`:

  sbagenx -G -p sigmoid t40,10,5 -01ds+:l=0.125:h=0
  sbagenx --graph-video sigmoid.mp4 --graph-video-fps 30 \
          -G -p sigmoid t40,10,5 -01ds+:l=0.125:h=0

This writes a PNG graph of the beat/pulse curve over the drop period
only, then exits.  The filename is generated from the sigmoid
parameters, with unsupported filename characters replaced.

The graph includes labelled axes and numeric tick marks.  The X-axis
shows time in minutes (0 to D over the drop period).  The Y-axis
shows beat/pulse frequency in Hz, with tick values snapped to readable
whole-number steps.  Rendering is supersampled for
smoother curves and cleaner line quality.  The bottom caption also
prints the active sigmoid parameters (start/target, D, l/h, and solved
a/b values) for reference.

`-G` first tries the Python+Cairo backend (bundled by default in current
Windows builds).  If not available, it falls back to the built-in
renderer.

`--graph-video` requires the Python+Cairo backend plus `ffmpeg` with
`libx264` support, and writes an MP4 animation of the plotted drop
curve with audio for the same plotted period.  If `ffmpeg` or
`libx264` is missing, SBaGenX reports that graph video export requires
an `ffmpeg` build with `libx264` installed and available on `PATH`.

Compatibility fallback: `-P` also triggers this program graph when no
cycle-plot target is present (`-H` absent and no `mixam:<...>` extra).


2.10.1: More detailed notes on -p sigmoid options
-------------------------------------------------

Usage:

  -p sigmoid [t<drop>,<hold>,<wake>]
      <signed-level|N><target-spec>[s|k][+][^][@|M][/<amp>]
      [:l=<val>][:h=<val>][:S=<val>] <tone-specs> ...
  -G -p sigmoid [t<drop>,<hold>,<wake>]
      <signed-level|N><target-spec>[s|k][+][^][@|M][/<amp>]
      [:l=<val>][:h=<val>][:S=<val>]

Examples:

  -p sigmoid 00a
  -p sigmoid 00ds+:l=0.125:h=0
  -p sigmoid 20fs+^/2:h=0:l=0.125 mix/98
  -p sigmoid 34.5dk+/1.5:l=0.20:h=-2 pink/30
  -p sigmoid -01ds+@/3:l=0.125:h=0 brown/15
  -p sigmoid 00ds+M/2:l=0.125:h=0
  -p sigmoid 00ls+:l=0.125:h=0:S=2 mix/100 mixam:beat
  -p sigmoid Nls+:l=0.125:h=0:S=2 mix/100 mixam:beat
  -A -p sigmoid 00ls+/3:l=0.125:h=0 mix/97
  -A d=0.3:e=0.3:k=10:E=0.7 -p sigmoid 00ls+/3:l=0.125:h=0 mix/97

Level and carrier behavior:

  Let L = signed level value
  Starting carrier (Hz):   f_start = 205 - 2*L
  Reverse lookup:          L = (205 - f_start) / 2

Using `N` in place of the signed level mutes generated program tones
while still generating the beat/pulse trajectory (for example to drive
`mixam:beat` without adding a separate binaural/isochronic/monaural tone).

As with `-p drop`, the carrier falls by 5Hz at t=D.  If `+` is present,
it continues to drop over hold at the same linear rate as in drop, for
an extra hold drop of:

  5 * (hold-time / drop-time) Hz

(before any wake-up `^`).

Beat/pulse depth behavior:

The target spec selects the same beat/pulse endpoint used by `-p drop`.
Base letters `a-l` map from 4.4 down to 0.3, fractional forms (for
example `a.5`) interpolate toward the next deeper target, and negative
forms (for example `-b`) mirror the same spacing upward around
`a = 4.4Hz`.  The beat/pulse always starts at 10Hz unless overridden by
`:S=<value>`, then moves to the selected target over the drop period.

Reference (lowest beat/pulse target by letter):
  +--------+-----+-----+-----+-----+-----+-----+
  | Letter |  a  |  b  |  c  |  d  |  e  |  f  |
  +--------+-----+-----+-----+-----+-----+-----+
  | Hz     | 4.4 | 3.7 | 3.1 | 2.5 | 2.0 | 1.5 |
  +--------+-----+-----+-----+-----+-----+-----+
  | Letter |  g  |  h  |  i  |  j  |  k  |  l  |
  +--------+-----+-----+-----+-----+-----+-----+
  | Hz     | 1.2 | 0.9 | 0.7 | 0.5 | 0.4 | 0.3 |
  +--------+-----+-----+-----+-----+-----+-----+

Reference (mirrored negative target by letter):
  +--------+------+------+------+------+------+------+
  | Letter | -a   | -b   | -c   | -d   | -e   | -f   |
  +--------+------+------+------+------+------+------+
  | Hz     | 4.4  | 5.1  | 5.7  | 6.3  | 6.8  | 7.3  |
  +--------+------+------+------+------+------+------+
  | Letter | -g   | -h   | -i   | -j   | -k   | -l   |
  +--------+------+------+------+------+------+------+
  | Hz     | 7.6  | 7.9  | 8.1  | 8.3  | 8.4  | 8.5  |
  +--------+------+------+------+------+------+------+

Sigmoid shape parameters:

The optional shape parameters are:
  :l=<value>   (default 0.125)
  :h=<value>   (default 0)
  :S=<value>   (default 10.0, start beat/pulse in Hz)

Order does not matter.  Either can be omitted, and both can be used
together.  Time for these parameters is interpreted in minutes.

For `-p sigmoid ...s...`, the beat/pulse follows:

  f(t) = a * tanh( l * (t - D/2 - h) ) + b

Where:
  t = time in minutes from start of drop
  D = drop duration in minutes
  l, h = user shape parameters

Boundary conditions:
  f(0) = S (default 10.0, configurable via :S=<value>)
  f(D) = target from target spec

`a` and `b` are solved automatically from those boundary conditions.

Step/slide options:

By default, frequencies step every 3 minutes.  With `k`, they step
every 1 minute.  With `s`, the frequencies slide continuously and the
sigmoid shape is applied directly over time.

Other options:

  +   add hold period after drop
  ^   add wake-up segment at end
  @   use isochronic pulse instead of binaural beat
  M   use monaural beat mode instead of binaural beat
  /x  set amplitude (default 1.0)

With `M` in the spec, `-p sigmoid` uses:

  f1 = carrier - beat/2
  f2 = carrier + beat/2

Each ear receives both tones.  `@` and monaural mode are mutually
exclusive.
The same target-spec and timing rules apply to monaural frequency
instead of binaural beat frequency.

Mix modulation parameters (via `-A`):

Rationale (conjectural): the linear decay term `V(t)` provides gradual
quietening that may feel soothing, while the periodic dip term `G(t)`
can intermittently heighten awareness.
Reference graph: assets/example-mix-amplitude-modulation.png
(generated for t30,30,0 with defaults d=0.3, e=0.3, k=10, E=0.7).

  -A                 use defaults: d=0.3 e=0.3 k=10 E=0.7
  -A d=<v>:e=<v>:k=<v>:E=<v>   override selected parameters

For main session time (`drop + hold`, excluding wake):

  G(t) = 1 - d * exp( -e * ((t mod 2k) - k)^2 )
  V(t) = 1 - ((1 - E) / T) * t
  F(t) = G(t) * V(t)

Note: period/tone-set displays still show the base `mix/<amp>` value.
When `-A` is active, the runtime status line shows `mix/<value>` as the
effective mix amplitude after modulation.

For wake-up time (if `^` is present):

  W(t) = E + ((1 - E) / U) * t

If there is no wake-up phase, the modulation holds at `E` after the
main session instead of jumping back to full level.

All times in these equations are in minutes.

Extra tone-specs after the sigmoid spec are supported (for example
mix/pink/white/brown overlays), just like in `-p drop`.


2.10.2: Adjusting the length of a -p sigmoid session
----------------------------------------------------

You can put an optional time specification between `sigmoid` and the
sigmoid spec:

  t<drop-time>,<hold-time>,<wake-time>

For example:

  t40,10,5

This gives a 40-minute drop, optional 10-minute hold (if `+` is used),
and optional 5-minute wake-up (if `^` is used).  Example:

  sbagenx -m river2.ogg -p sigmoid t40,10,5 20ds+^/2:l=0.125:h=0 mix/98

As with `-p drop`, built-in `-p` programs are finite by default and
stop automatically at their programmed end time.  Lengths are
whole-minute based.  In stepped (`k`) mode, the internal checkpoints
still follow the normal grid, but the overall programmed runtime is
not extended to match that grid.


2.11: The 'curve' sequences
---------------------------

This is advanced functionality.  If you are new to SBaGenX sequence
syntax, read section 3 first, then return here.

`-p curve` lets you drive built-in sessions from expressions stored in
an external function file.  The file extension is `.sbgf` (required).
Like the other built-in `-p` programs, these sessions are finite by
default and stop automatically at their programmed end time unless
`-L` overrides the output length.

Usage:

  -p curve <curve-file.sbgf> [t<drop>,<hold>,<wake>]
      <signed-level|N><target-spec>[s|k][+][^][@|M][/<amp>][:S=<val>]
      [:name=value ...]
      <tone-specs> ...

In `<curve-spec>`, `:S=<val>` sets the start beat/pulse frequency in Hz
(default 10.0).  Each `:name=value` token overrides a matching
`param name = ...` in the `.sbgf` file (except reserved `S`).
The names are generic, so `l`/`h` are only examples used by some curve
files.

Examples:

  sbagenx -p curve examples/basics/curve-sigmoid-like.sbgf \
    00ls:l=0.2:h=0
  sbagenx -p curve examples/basics/curve-sigmoid-like.sbgf \
    00ls:S=2:l=0.2:h=0
  sbagenx -p curve examples/basics/curve-piecewise-demo.sbgf \
    t30,30,0 00ls+:p1=8:p2=18
  sbagenx -m river1.ogg \
    -p curve examples/basics/curve-amp-mix-demo.sbgf 00ls+ mix/97
  sbagenx -m river1.ogg \
    -p curve examples/basics/curve-sigmoid-like.sbgf 00ls+ mix/99
  sbagenx -m river1.ogg \
    -p curve examples/basics/curve-sigmoid-like.sbgf \
    Nls:S=2:l=0.2:h=0 mix/100 mixam:beat
  sbagenx -G -p curve examples/basics/curve-sigmoid-like.sbgf \
    00ls:l=0.2:h=0
  sbagenx -G -p curve examples/basics/curve-smootherstep-demo.sbgf \
    00ls
  sbagenx -G -p curve examples/basics/curve-raised-cosine-demo.sbgf \
    00ls
  sbagenx -G -p curve examples/basics/curve-easeout-demo.sbgf \
    00ls
  sbagenx -G -p curve examples/basics/curve-expfit-solve-demo.sbgf \
    00ls:l=0.15
  sbagenx -G -p curve examples/basics/curve-3unk-double-exp-solve-demo.sbgf \
    00ls:l=0.06:k=0.20:s0=0

The installer ships curated example curve plots in the `Plots`
sub-directory alongside these matching `.sbgf` examples.

The level/depth/time modifiers are interpreted the same way as in
`-p drop`/`-p sigmoid`:

  - Signed level selects starting carrier baseline.
  - `N` mutes generated program tones but keeps beat/pulse trajectory active.
  - Target spec selects target beat/pulse endpoint (`b1`).
  - `s` = continuous runtime curve evaluation.
  - `k` or default = stepped checkpoints sampled from the expression.
  - `+`, `^`, `@`, `M`, `/amp` behave as in other built-in programs.

For `amp` and `mixamp`:
  - In `s` mode, values are evaluated continuously at runtime.
  - In stepped mode (`k` or default), values are sampled per checkpoint.

`@` (isochronic) and `M` (monaural) are mutually exclusive.

With `-G`, `-p curve` plots the evaluated beat/pulse frequency curve
over the drop period (`D`) and exits.

`--graph-video file.mp4` may be added with `-G` to write an MP4
animation of the same curve with a moving cursor, pulsating dot, and
the plotted-period audio track muxed into the video.

Compatibility fallback: `-P` also triggers this program graph when no
cycle-plot target is present (`-H` absent and no `mixam:<...>` extra).


2.11.1: The .sbgf function file format
--------------------------------------

Supported line types:

  param <name> = <number>[m|s] (optional unit suffix: m=minutes, s=seconds)
  solve <u1>,<u2>,... : <lhs1>=<rhs1> ; <lhs2>=<rhs2> ; ... (optional)
  beat = <expression>          (required)
  carrier = <expression>       (optional)
  amp = <expression>           (optional, beat/iso/mono amplitude %,
                                0..100 scale)
  mixamp = <expression>        (optional, mix/<amp> amplitude %, 0..100 scale)
  mixspin_width = <expression> (optional, mixspin width in microseconds)
  mixspin_hz = <expression>    (optional, mixspin rotation frequency in Hz)
  mixspin_amp = <expression>   (optional, mixspin intensity %, 0..100 scale)
  mixpulse_hz = <expression>   (optional, mixpulse rate in Hz)
  mixpulse_amp = <expression>  (optional, mixpulse intensity %, 0..100 scale)
  mixbeat_hz = <expression>    (optional, mixbeat frequency in Hz)
  mixbeat_amp = <expression>   (optional, mixbeat intensity %, 0..100 scale)
  mixam_hz = <expression>      (optional, mixam rate in Hz)
  beat<expr = <expression>     (piecewise, optional)
  beat<=expr = <expression>    (piecewise, optional)
  beat>expr = <expression>     (piecewise, optional)
  beat>=expr = <expression>    (piecewise, optional)
  carrier<expr = <expression>  (piecewise, optional)
  carrier<=expr = <expression> (piecewise, optional)
  carrier>expr = <expression>  (piecewise, optional)
  carrier>=expr = <expression> (piecewise, optional)
  amp<expr = <expression>      (piecewise, optional)
  amp<=expr = <expression>     (piecewise, optional)
  amp>expr = <expression>      (piecewise, optional)
  amp>=expr = <expression>     (piecewise, optional)
  mixamp<expr = <expression>   (piecewise, optional)
  mixamp<=expr = <expression>  (piecewise, optional)
  mixamp>expr = <expression>   (piecewise, optional)
  mixamp>=expr = <expression>  (piecewise, optional)
  mixspin_width<expr = <expression>  (piecewise, optional)
  ... same < <= > >= piecewise forms also apply to:
      mixspin_hz, mixspin_amp,
      mixpulse_hz, mixpulse_amp,
      mixbeat_hz, mixbeat_amp,
      mixam_hz

Comments can start with `#`, `;` or `//`.
Each expression must be on a single line.
For each target (`beat`, `carrier`, `amp`, `mixamp`, and the optional
mix-effect targets listed above), use either:
  1) one plain expression line (`beat = ...`), or
  2) piecewise lines (`beat<...`, `beat>...`, etc).
Mixing plain and piecewise lines for the same target is not allowed.

`solve` is evaluated once at setup time (not per sample).  It solves
constants/parameters so your runtime formulas can stay simple.
Current implementation supports one `solve` line per file and requires
a square system (`#equations == #unknowns`).

Expression variables available at runtime:

  t    seconds from start of main session
  m    minutes from start of main session
  D    drop-time in minutes
  H    hold-time in minutes
  T    total main time in minutes (drop + hold)
  U    wake-time in minutes
  b0   start beat/pulse frequency in Hz (normally 10.0)
  b1   target beat/pulse frequency in Hz (from target spec)
  c0   start carrier frequency in Hz
  c1   end carrier frequency in Hz (after drop/hold)
  a0   base beat/iso/mono amplitude in % (0..100, from /amp in curve spec)
  m0   base mix amplitude in % (0..100, from mix/<amp> tone-spec)

`amp`, `mixamp`, `mixspin_amp`, `mixpulse_amp`, and `mixbeat_amp`
expressions return percentages on a 0..100 scale
(not fractions in the 0.0..1.0 range).
For example: 100 = full configured amplitude, 50 = half amplitude.
`mixamp` affects runtime mix amplitude only when a `mix/<amp>` tone-spec
is present.
The mix-effect targets affect runtime mix-effect parameters only when a
matching mix effect is present.  They do not create mix effects by
themselves.

Matching rules:

  mixspin_width / mixspin_hz / mixspin_amp  -> active `mixspin`
  mixpulse_hz / mixpulse_amp                -> active `mixpulse`
  mixbeat_hz / mixbeat_amp                  -> active `mixbeat`
  mixam_hz                                  -> active `mixam`

`mixam_hz` overrides the effective AM rate in Hz.  If a curve supplies
`mixam_hz`, it takes precedence over `mixam:beat` binding for that
evaluated runtime spec.

Helper functions (implemented in C):

  ifelse(cond,a,b), step(x), clamp(x,lo,hi), lerp(a,b,u)
  ramp(x,x0,x1), smoothstep(edge0,edge1,x), smootherstep(edge0,edge1,x)
  between(x,a,b), pulse(x,start,end), seg(x,x0,x1,y0,y1)
  lt(a,b), le(a,b), gt(a,b), ge(a,b), eq(a,b), ne(a,b)
  min2(a,b), max2(a,b)

Logarithm convention in `.sbgf` expressions:

  ln(x)   natural logarithm (base e)
  log(x)  base-10 logarithm

If you are pairing with `exp(...)` (for example in solved exponential
curves), use `ln(...)`.

Quick reference:

  ifelse(cond,a,b)
    Returns a if cond is non-zero, otherwise b.

  step(x)
    Returns 1 when x >= 0, else 0.

  lt/le/gt/ge/eq/ne(a,b)
    Comparison helpers that return 1 (true) or 0 (false).

  clamp(x,lo,hi)
    Clamps x into the [lo, hi] range.
    If x <= lo, returns lo.
    If x >= hi, returns hi.
    If lo < x < hi, returns x.
    If lo > hi, bounds are swapped internally.
    Examples:
      clamp(12,0,10) = 10
      clamp(-3,0,10) = 0
      clamp(5,0,10) = 5

  lerp(a,b,u)
    Linear interpolation from a to b by factor u.
    Formula: a + (b-a)*u
    (u=0 -> a, u=1 -> b)
    Note: lerp does not clamp u.
    Examples:
      lerp(10,20,0.25) = 12.5
      lerp(10,20,1.5) = 25
      lerp(10,20,clamp(1.5,0,1)) = 20

  ramp(x,x0,x1)
    Normalized linear ramp from 0 to 1 between x0 and x1,
    clamped outside that interval.
    Examples:
      ramp(2,5,15) = 0
      ramp(10,5,15) = 0.5
      ramp(20,5,15) = 1
    Practical use:
      lerp(10,4,ramp(m,0,30))
      gives a linear drop from 10 to 4 over 30 minutes.

  smoothstep(edge0,edge1,x)
    Smoothed 0..1 transition (cubic easing) between edge0 and edge1.
    Internal normalized variable:
      u = ramp(x,edge0,edge1)
    Formula:
      smoothstep = u*u*(3 - 2*u)
    Qualitative behavior:
      starts gently, speeds up in the middle, then slows gently again.

  smootherstep(edge0,edge1,x)
    Even smoother 0..1 transition (quintic easing).
    Internal normalized variable:
      u = ramp(x,edge0,edge1)
    Formula:
      smootherstep = u*u*u*(u*(u*6 - 15) + 10)
    Qualitative behavior:
      similar to smoothstep, but with even softer entry/exit.

  between(x,a,b)
    Inclusive range test: 1 when x is between a and b, else 0.

  pulse(x,start,end)
    Alias for between(x,start,end).

  seg(x,x0,x1,y0,y1)
    Linear segment from (x0,y0) to (x1,y1), clamped at both ends.
    Useful for piecewise curves.

  min2/max2(a,b)
    min2(a,b) returns the smaller of a and b.
    max2(a,b) returns the larger of a and b.
    Examples:
      min2(3,7) = 3
      max2(3,7) = 7
    Practical use:
      max2(b1, lerp(b0,b1,ramp(m,0,D)))
      keeps the value from dropping below b1.
      min2(10, expr) can cap expr at 10.

Examples:

  beat = lerp(b0,b1,ramp(m,0,D))
  beat = ifelse(lt(m,10), b0, b1)
  beat = b0 + 2 * pulse(m,5,7)

Parameter names in `.sbgf` are case-sensitive.  Values can be
overridden from the command line using `:name=value` tokens in the
curve spec.  These names are not fixed keywords; they are user-defined
`param` names from the `.sbgf` file.  Order does not matter.

`solve` unknown names use the same naming rules as `param` names.
If not already defined with `param`, they are created automatically
with initial value `0`.  You can still provide `:name=value` overrides
to change initial guesses before solving.

Common `solve` errors:

  1) Non-square system (not currently supported):
       solve A,C : A+C=b0
     Here, unknowns=2 and equations=1.

  2) Missing ':' separator:
       solve A,C A+C=b0 ; A-C=b1
     Correct form is:
       solve A,C : A+C=b0 ; A-C=b1

  3) Missing '=' in one equation chunk:
       solve A,C : A+C ; A-C=b1
     Here the first chunk (`A+C`) has no '='.
     Every semicolon-separated chunk must be <lhs>=<rhs>.

  4) Duplicate unknown names:
       solve A,A : A+C=b0 ; A-C=b1
     Unknown names must be unique.

Valid minimal example:

  solve A,C : A*exp(-l*0)+C=b0 ; A*exp(-l*D)+C=b1

Parameter unit suffixes:
  - `m` means minutes
  - `s` means seconds (internally converted to minutes)
  - no suffix keeps previous behavior (plain numeric value)

Piecewise line behavior:
  - Conditions are checked in file order.
  - The first matching condition is used.
  - If none match, the last piecewise expression for that target is used
    as the fallback.

Example `.sbgf` (sigmoid-like beat with optional carrier override):

  param l = 0.125
  param h = 0
  beat = b0 + (b1-b0) * (0.5 + 0.5*tanh(l*(m-D/2-h)))
  carrier = c0 + (c1-c0) * ramp(m,0,T)
  amp = a0 * (1 - 0.4*ramp(m,0,D))
  mixamp = m0 * (1 - 0.3*ramp(m,0,T))

Example `.sbgf` piecewise beat:

  param p1 = 8m
  param p2 = 18m
  beat<p1 = seg(m,0,p1,b0,6)
  beat<p2 = seg(m,p1,p2,6,3)
  beat>=p2 = seg(m,p2,D,3,b1)

The same example in seconds:

  param p1 = 480s
  param p2 = 1080s
  beat<p1 = seg(m,0,p1,b0,6)
  beat<p2 = seg(m,p1,p2,6,3)
  beat>=p2 = seg(m,p2,D,3,b1)

Example `.sbgf` using auto-solved constants:

  # Target model: beat(m) = A*exp(-l*m) + C
  param l = 0.15
  solve A,C : A*exp(-l*0)+C=b0 ; A*exp(-l*D)+C=b1
  beat = A*exp(-l*m) + C
  carrier = c0 + (c1-c0) * ramp(m,0,T)

Example `.sbgf` with 3 unknowns solved:

  # Target model: beat(m) = A*exp(-l*m) + B*exp(-k*m) + C
  # Constraints:
  #   beat(0)=b0, beat(D)=b1, beat'(0)=s0
  param l = 0.06
  param k = 0.20
  param s0 = 0
  solve A,B,C : A+B+C=b0 ; A*exp(-l*D)+B*exp(-k*D)+C=b1 ; -l*A-k*B=s0
  beat = A*exp(-l*m) + B*exp(-k*m) + C
  carrier = c0 + (c1-c0) * ramp(m,0,T)

Example `.sbgf` driving runtime mix effects:

  beat = b0*exp(ln(b1/b0)*(m/D))
  carrier = c0 + (c1-c0)*ramp(m,0,T)
  mixspin_width = 300 + 60*ramp(m,0,T)
  mixspin_hz = 3 + 1*ramp(m,0,T)
  mixspin_amp = 35 + 15*ramp(m,0,T)
  mixam_hz = 2 + 2*ramp(m,0,T)

  Example use:
    sbagenx -p curve examples/basics/curve-mixfx-demo.sbgf \
      00ls+ mix/80 mixspin:300+3/35
    sbagenx -p curve examples/basics/curve-mixfx-demo.sbgf \
      Nls+ mix/100 mixam:beat


3: Writing sequence files
-------------------------

If you decide you would like to create your own sequences, then you
need to learn how to write a sequence file.  Probably the easiest way
of starting is by looking at existing sequence files (the prog-*
files), and then take a copy of one that seems close and then modify
it until it does what you want.  For example, 'prog-tape-3.sbg' is a
good example of a sequence that gently slides between several
brainwave frequencies using a single binaural tone.

For Windows and macOS users, you can edit these files using EDIT or
NotePad/TextEdit (or any simple text editor).  Mac users will have to
use an editor that respects UNIX line-endings (e.g. 'emacs', BBEdit, or
whatever).


3.1: The sequence-file format
-----------------------------

Within the sequence file, blank lines, and any leading/trailing white
space are ignored.  Comments are introduced with a '#', and extend to
the end of the line.

Comments starting with two hashes '##' are special because they are
printed out when the sequence is run.  They can be used to include a
description of the sequence (or credits or whatever) that is visible
to users of the sequence.

Apart from comments and blank lines, there are several types of
entries that may appear in the file:

  * Command-line options
  * Tone-set definition lines
  * Time-sequence lines
  * Block definition lines

These are described below.  Take a look at the example sequence-files
(prog-*).  They are useful to see how all these work together.


3.1.1: Command-line options within the sequence file
----------------------------------------------------

All of the command-line options reported by 'sbagenx -h' can also be
included in sequence files.  They should appear at the top, before any
tone-sets or whatever.  The purpose of these options are to permit
soundtracks to be specified, and to indicate whether the sequence is a
24-hour sequence or an on-demand sequence.

Some of the options that are useful inside a sequence-file are as
follows:

  -Q        Quiet - don't display running status
  -S        Output from the first tone-set time in the sequence (Start),
             instead of running according to the clock
  -E        Output until the last tone-set in the sequence (End),
             instead of outputting forever
  -T time   Start at the given clock-time (hh:mm)
  -L time   Select the length of time (hh:mm or hh:mm:ss) to output for
  -m file   Read audio data from the given file and mix it with the
              generated binaural beats; may be flac/ogg/mp3/wav/raw format
  -A [spec] Enable mix amplitude modulation for -p drop/-p sigmoid.
              Optional spec: d=<v>:e=<v>:k=<v>:E=<v>
              Requires a mix input stream via -m or -M.
  -P        Plot one-cycle envelope/waveform graph as PNG and exit.
              With -H: renders one-cycle mixam envelope/gain.
              With -p ... and mixam:<...> extras: renders one-cycle
              mixam envelope/gain using current/default -H parameters.
              Otherwise: renders one-cycle isochronic (@) waveform
              using current tone and `-I` settings.
              Compatibility: with -p drop/-p sigmoid/-p curve and no
              cycle target, falls back to the program beat/pulse graph.
              Python+Cairo backend is preferred; otherwise built-in
              plotting is used.
  -G        Plot program beat/pulse-vs-time graph as PNG and exit.
              Supported with -p drop/-p sigmoid/-p curve.
            --graph-video file
              With -G, also write an MP4 graph animation with audio.
              Requires Python+Cairo backend plus ffmpeg with libx264.
            --graph-video-fps n
              Frame rate for --graph-video (default 30).
  -I [spec] Customize isochronic (@) pulse envelope.
              Optional spec:
                s=<start-cycle>:d=<duty>:a=<attack>:r=<release>:e=<edge>
              Defaults: s=0.0485, d=0.4030, a=0.5, r=0.5, e=2.
              Constraint: a+r <= 1.
              e modes: 0=hard, 1=linear, 2=smoothstep, 3=smootherstep.
  -H [spec] Customize mixam envelope globally.
              Optional spec:
                m=<pulse|cos>:s=<start-cycle>:d=<duty>:a=<attack>:
                r=<release>:e=<edge>:f=<floor>
              Order does not matter. Omitted parameters use defaults.
              Defaults: m=cos, s=0, d=0.5, a=0.5, r=0.5, e=3, f=0.45.
              Compatibility: if d/a/r/e are supplied without m=...,
              pulse mode is implied.
              In m=cos mode, d/a/r/e are ignored (continuous raised-cosine AM).
              Constraint: in m=pulse mode, a+r <= 1.
              e modes: 0=hard, 1=linear, 2=smoothstep, 3=smootherstep.

  -i ...    Generate the whole sequence from an 'immediate' spec
  -p ...    Generate the whole sequence from a predefined sequence type,
              e.g. -p drop or -p slide
  -V level  Set global volume level (1-100, default 100)
  -w type   Set waveform type: sine, square, triangle, sawtooth (default sine)
  -N        Disable automatic amplitude normalization

The option and its arguments must appear on a line together, and
several options may be put on one line if you wish, or they may be
spread over several lines.

For example, it is common to use -SE to indicate a sequence that runs
from start to end and then stops:

  -SE

To mix with a particular background soundtrack, use -m:

  -m soundtrack.mp3

Note that if the user runs sbagen with another -m option, the user's
command-line option overrides the one in the sequence file.

To run for 30 minutes from the clock-time of 2pm in the sequence file,
try this:

  -L 0:30 -T 14:00

Additional SBaGenX options can also be used in sequence files:

To set a custom global volume level (useful for creating quieter sequences):

  -V 50

To use a different waveform type for all binaural beats and isochronic tones:

  -w triangle

To disable automatic amplitude normalization (allows amplitude totals > 100%):

  -N


3.1.2: Tone-set definition lines
--------------------------------

The purpose of this is to describe a set of binaural tones, pink noise
or whatever which are played together at any one time.  Once they are
defined, you can slide from one to the next, or fade them in and out
in the following section.

A tone-set definition line takes the form:

    <name>: <tone-spec> <tone-spec> ...

Examples:

  theta6: pink/40 150+6/30
  test10: pink/40 100+1.5/30 200-4/27 400+8/1.5
  all-off: -
  
  # Examples using individual waveforms:
  alpha-square: square:200+10/20 pink/30
  mixed-iso: sine:300@8/15 triangle:150@12/10
  energizing: square:400+15/30 sawtooth:600+20/15 white/10
  spatial-mix: mix/80 triangle:mixspin:400+6.0/40
  rhythmic-mix: mix/75 square:mixpulse:8/50
  binauralized-mix: mix/80 mixbeat:6/45
  am-mix: mix/80 mixam:8

The name starts with a letter, and may continue with letters, numbers,
'_' or '-'.  Following the colon is a list of one or more tone
specifications.  There are a maximum of 16 of these.  (This can be
adjusted in the source code -- search for N_CH).

Here are the different types:

  -                                   # Channel not in use
  pink/<amp>                          # Pink noise
  white/<amp>                         # White noise
  brown/<amp>                         # Brown noise
  mix/<amp>                           # Soundtrack input mix
  <carrier><sign><freq>/<amp>         # Binaural tones
  <carrier>/<amp>                      # Sine-wave (a binaural tone with
                                       # 0Hz beat)
  bell+<freq>/<amp>                   # Bell sound
  spin:<width>+<freq>/<amp>           # Spinning variable-delay pink noise
  bspin:<width>+<freq>/<amp>          # Spinning variable-delay brown noise
  wspin:<width>+<freq>/<amp>          # Spinning variable-delay white noise
  mixspin:<width>+<freq>/<intensity>  # Spinning effect on mix input
  mixpulse:<pulse>/<intensity>        # Pulse effect on mix input
  mixbeat:<beat>/<intensity>          # Hilbert-shifted binauralized mix
  mixam:<pulse|beat>                # Pure AM on mix input (envelope via -H)
  <carrier>@<pulse>/<amp>             # Isochronic tones
  <waveform>:<carrier><sign><freq>/<amp>
                                       # Binaural tones with specific
                                       # waveform
  <waveform>:<carrier>@<pulse>/<amp>  # Isochronic tones with specific waveform
  <waveform>:<carrier>/<amp>          # Simple tones with specific waveform
  <waveform>:mixspin:<width>+<freq>/<intensity>
                                       # Mixspin with specific waveform
                                       # modulation
  <waveform>:mixpulse:<pulse>/<intensity>
                                       # Mixpulse with specific waveform
                                       # modulation
  <waveform>:mixbeat:<beat>/<intensity>
                                       # Mixbeat with waveform modulation

The amplitudes are expressed on a scale 0-100.  By default, SBaGenX 
automatically normalizes amplitudes when the total exceeds 100% to prevent 
audio distortion. If you want to disable this behavior and allow clipping 
(which may cause distortion), you can use the -N option. In the original 
behavior (with -N), if the total of all amplitudes <amp> in a tone-set 
exceeds 100, there will be distortion on output as the waveforms are clipped.

The binaural tones are based on a carrier frequency (in Hz), which
should typically be in the range 50 to 1000.  Frequencies above
1000-1500 or so are of no use because the brain does not react to
higher pitches binaurally in the same way (see the Monroe Institute
pages).  You can go down to 50Hz and below if you like, but make sure
that your headphones actually go that low first.  The actual beat
frequency <freq> (which the brain responds to) should typically be in
the range 0.5 to 30 Hz.  This corresponds to the ranges for Delta up
to Beta as described above.

The two tones generated are different by <freq>Hz, equally spaced
either side of <carrier>.  The <sign> should be + or -, and this
decides which channel carries the higher frequency.  I don't think
this matters, but in case it does, you can change it here.  So, for
example, 100+4/20 generates tones of 102 and 98 Hz, whereas 100-4/20
generates tones of 98 and 102 Hz.

**Individual Waveform Specifications**

SBaGenX supports specifying individual waveforms for each tone in a
tone-set definition, allowing you to use different waveform types
(sine, square, triangle, sawtooth) for specific tones rather than
applying a global waveform with the -w option.

The syntax for individual waveform specifications is:

  <waveform>:<tone-spec>

where <waveform> can be one of: sine, square, triangle, sawtooth

Examples:

  sine:200+10/20          # Binaural beats using sine wave
  square:300@8/15         # Isochronic tones using square wave  
  triangle:150-6/25       # Binaural beats using triangle wave
  sawtooth:400/10         # Simple tone using sawtooth wave
  triangle:mixspin:400+6.0/40  # Mixspin with triangle wave modulation
  square:mixpulse:8/60    # Mixpulse with square wave modulation
  sine:mixbeat:6/45       # Mixbeat with sine modulation

This feature allows for precise control over the waveform
characteristics of individual elements within a tone-set. For example,
you might want to use a sine wave for the main binaural beat while
using a square wave for an isochronic pulse:

  mixed-waves: sine:200+10/15 square:300@4/10 pink/20

**Waveform Characteristics:**

- **Sine Wave**: Pure fundamental frequency, smooth and gentle, least fatiguing
- **Square Wave**: Contains odd harmonics (3rd, 5th, 7th, etc.), more
  penetrating 
  and aggressive, useful for alertness and concentration
- **Triangle Wave**: Contains odd harmonics with faster decay than
  square wave, 
  providing a middle ground between sine and square
- **Sawtooth Wave**: Contains both odd and even harmonics, creating a bright, 
  buzzy sound, useful for energizing effects

When individual waveforms are specified, they override any global waveform 
setting from the -w command-line option for that specific tone. Tones without 
explicit waveform specifications will use the global setting (default: sine).

The waveform specification can be applied to:
- Binaural beats (<carrier><sign><freq>/<amp>)
- Isochronic tones (<carrier>@<pulse>/<amp>)
- Simple tones (<carrier>/<amp>)
- Mixspin effects (triangle:mixspin:<width>+<freq>/<intensity>)
- Mixpulse effects (square:mixpulse:<pulse>/<intensity>)
- Mixbeat effects (sine:mixbeat:<beat>/<intensity>)

Note: For mixspin/mixpulse/mixbeat effects, the waveform modulates the
effect envelope. `customNN:` may also be used for these three mix effects
to provide a hand-shaped unipolar effect envelope. This does not extend to
`spin`, `bspin`, or `wspin`, whose modulation is inherently bipolar.
For mixbeat, a sine waveform is recommended for the cleanest spectral result.
Note: mixam uses explicit envelope parameters (m,s,d,a,r,e,f) via `-H` (or
inline in the token for compatibility), and does not use waveform prefixes
for shaping.

The isochronic tones use amplitude modulation of a single carrier frequency
to create a pulsing effect. The <carrier> is the frequency of the tone in Hz,
<pulse> is the pulse rate in Hz (typically in the range 0.5 to 30 Hz, 
corresponding to the brain wave frequencies), and <amp> is the amplitude
as with other tone types. For example, 300@10/20 generates a 300Hz tone
that pulses at 10Hz with an amplitude of 20%. Unlike binaural beats, 
isochronic tones can be effective even when not using headphones, as they
don't rely on the brain combining two slightly different frequencies.

The white noise generates a random signal with equal energy at all frequencies.
It can be used for masking background sounds or as a meditation aid. The
amplitude <amp> controls the volume level. For example, white/10 generates
white noise at 10% amplitude.

The brown noise (also known as Brownian noise) has more energy in the lower
frequencies, creating a deeper, more bass-heavy sound than pink noise. It's
often described as similar to the sound of a waterfall or heavy rainfall.
The amplitude <amp> controls the volume level. For example, brown/15 generates
brown noise at 15% amplitude.

The bell sound can be used to give cues and signals during the
sequence.  A simple 'ping' is generated at the given frequency at
the start of the period in which the tone-set is used.

The spinning pink noise generates a binaural effect in a completely
different way to binaural tones, by shifting the delay of pink noise
in opposite directions for each ear, at a given beat frequency.  In
this case, the first number is the width of the maximum delay from
centre in microseconds.  Values from 100 to 500 seem good to me,
although you might want to experiment with this.  The second number is
the beat frequency and the third is the amplitude, as above.

The spinning brown noise generates a binaural effect in a completely
different way to binaural tones, by shifting the delay of brown noise
in opposite directions for each ear, at a given beat frequency.  In
this case, the first number is the width of the maximum delay from
centre in microseconds.  Values from 100 to 500 seem good to me,
although you might want to experiment with this.  The second number is
the beat frequency and the third is the amplitude, as above.

The spinning white noise generates a binaural effect in a completely
different way to binaural tones, by shifting the delay of white noise
in opposite directions for each ear, at a given beat frequency.  In
this case, the first number is the width of the maximum delay from
centre in microseconds.  Values from 100 to 500 seem good to me,
although you might want to experiment with this.  The second number is
the beat frequency and the third is the amplitude, as above.

The mixspin effect creates a spatial rotation effect on the audio input stream
(specified with mix/<amp>). The <width> parameter controls the amplitude of 
the rotation effect in microseconds (typically 100-500μs), <freq> determines 
the rotation speed in Hz, and <intensity> controls the intensity of the effect 
(0-100%). For example, mixspin:500+8.0/50 creates a spinning effect with 
500μs width, rotating at 8Hz with 50% intensity. This creates an immersive 
spatial effect where the sound appears to rotate around the listener's head.
The mixspin effect must be used in conjunction with a mix/<amp> specification
in the same tone set, as it processes the audio input rather than generating
its own tones. Higher intensity values create more pronounced rotation effects.

Waveforms can also be applied to mixspin effects to modulate the rotation 
intensity. For example, triangle:mixspin:400+6.0/40 applies a triangle wave 
modulation to the spinning effect, creating rhythmic variations in the 
spatial intensity.

The mixpulse effect creates a pulse effect on the audio input stream
(specified with mix/<amp>). The <pulse> parameter controls the pulse
rate in Hz, and <intensity> controls the intensity of the effect
(0-100%). For example, mixpulse:10/50 creates a pulse effect with a
rate of 10Hz and 50% intensity. This creates a rhythmic pulse effect
where the sound intensity fluctuates at the given rate.

Waveforms can also be applied to mixpulse effects to shape the pulse envelope.
For example, square:mixpulse:8/60 uses a square wave to create sharp on/off 
pulses, while sine:mixpulse:8/60 creates smoother pulsing variations.

The mixam effect applies true multiplicative amplitude modulation to the mix
stream: out(t)=mix(t)*mix_mul*g(t). The gain envelope g(t) is bounded in
[f,1] and is configured by:

Rationale: `mixam` was introduced to support broadband amplitude
modulation directly on the mix stream.  The motivation is that
broadband AM appears to have the strongest clinical support for
entraining very low frequencies, especially in the delta and
sub-delta ranges, whereas the evidence for binaural, monaural and
isochronic methods is weaker at those lowest frequencies.  `mixam`
therefore provides a way to keep the ambience or noise content of a
mix track while driving it with an explicit low-frequency envelope
instead of relying on a separate carrier-based beat signal.

This is especially relevant for `mixam:beat` used with a built-in `-p`
program: the programmed beat/pulse trajectory can still be followed
over time, but it is applied as broadband amplitude modulation of the
mix rather than as an additional binaural, monaural or isochronic
tone.

  m envelope model: cos (default, raised-cosine continuous AM) or pulse
  s phase offset in cycle (default 0)
  d duty in cycle (default 0.5)
  a attack share of ON window (default 0.5)
  r release share of ON window (default 0.5)
  e edge mode: 0 hard, 1 linear, 2 smoothstep, 3 smootherstep (default 3)
  f floor gain (default 0.45)
If d/a/r/e are supplied without m=..., pulse mode is implied for compatibility.
Constraint: in m=pulse mode, a+r must be <=1. Examples:
  -H s=0:d=0.5:a=0.1:r=0.1:e=3:f=0.25
  -H m=cos:s=0:f=0.45
  mix/80 mixam:8
  mix/80 mixam:beat
`mixam:<pulse>` uses a fixed AM frequency in Hz.
`mixam:beat` binds AM frequency to the current program beat/pulse value
for `-p` sessions, so it varies continuously as the program varies.
Compatibility alias: `mixpulse:beat[:params]` is accepted and treated as
`mixam:beat[:params]`.
You can still pass inline mixam params for compatibility, but when `-H` is
set, the `-H` envelope is applied globally to all mixam effects.
mixam is implemented in the sbagenxlib runtime extra-token path.

The mixbeat effect applies a Hilbert-transform frequency shift to the mix
stream (specified with mix/<amp>) and routes opposite shifts to left/right
channels to embed a binaural-style beat into existing audio. The <beat>
parameter is the target beat frequency in Hz and <intensity> controls wet
mix amount (0-100%). For example, mixbeat:6/45 creates a 6Hz embedded beat
at 45% effect intensity.

Waveforms can also be applied to mixbeat modulation. For example,
sine:mixbeat:6/45 is the clean default behavior; non-sine waveforms can
introduce additional sidebands and a more textured sound.

Binaural tones generated using wave<num> work just the same way as
normal binaural tones.  See the section later on for details.

Once a tone-set has been defined, it may be used in time-sequence
lines.


3.1.3: Time-sequence lines
--------------------------

Whereas the tone-set lines describe the different sets of tones that
should be played, the time-sequence lines describe *when* they should
be played, and how sbagen should move between them.

A time-sequence line takes one of the two forms:

  <time-spec> [<fade-in-fade-out>] <tone-set-name> [->]
  <time-spec> <block-name>

Examples:
  
  16:00 theta6
  NOW theta6
  +00:03:30 == theta4 ->
  NOW+04:00 <> theta4
  12:00 theta-bursts

In it's simplest form, this specifies a clock time, and which tone-set
should be played at that time.  This tone-set continues until the next
clock-time that is specified.

Time-sequence lines should always appear in order -- you will get an
error if things appear out of order.

More complex examples give time relative to the time that sbagen
started running (NOW, or NOW+04:00), or relative to the last absolute
time mentioned (+00:03:30).  They also use different varieties of
fade-in or fade-out (== or <>), and use '->' to slide to the next
time-sequence.  You can also name blocks (see 3.1.4) instead of
tone-sets.  These more complex options are described below.

Take a simple example such as this one:
  
  11:00 theta6
  12:00 alpha8
  13:00 alpha10
  14:00 off

This indicates three tone-sets that will play for an hour each between
11am and 2pm.  The tone-sets do not change suddenly on the hour.
Rather, sbagen starts to fade out one tone-set 30 secs before the
hour, fading in the next for the first 30 secs of the next hour.

By default, sbagen will attempt to keep common things playing from one
tone-set to the next.  So if, for example, all of them use pink noise
on the first channel, then this will keep on playing the whole time,
whilst the tones that change will fade out and in.

To change the fading in/out behaviour, include a <fade-in-fade-out>
specification before the name.  This consists of two characters, one
for fading in this tone-set, the second for fading it out.  The
fade-in character may be:

  <      Always fade in from silence.

  -      Fade from previous to this, fading out all tones not
         continuing, fading in all new tones, and adjusting the
         amplitude of any tones continuing.

  =      Slide from previous to this, fading out all tones ending,
         fading in all new tones, and sliding the frequency and
         adjusting the amplitude of all other tones.

As an example, using '=' you can smoothly slide from a 4 Hz frequency
all the way up to a 12 Hz frequency, rather than fading one out, and
fading a new one in.  The fade-out characters are similar, and may be:

  >      Fade out to silence
  -      Fade to next, as above
  =      Slide to next, as above

What fades/slides actually occur between two tone-sets depends on what
both of them are asking for.  If one wants to fade out to silence,
then the other one can't slide to it.  They both have to want to slide
for this to occur.

The default <fade-in-fade-out> specification is '--', which means keep
playing anything that is the same frequency (adjusting the amplitude
if necessary), but fade everything else in or out.

By default fading and sliding only occur during the 60 second period
within 30 seconds either side of the change-over time.  However, using
'->', the entire period from the start time to the time on the next
time-sequence line becomes a transition period.  This way you can have
a gentle frequency slide that goes on for an hour, if you like:

  15:00 == theta4 ->
  16:00 == alpha10

Assuming these tone-sets do what their names suggest, this will
produce a gentle change in binaural beat frequency from 4Hz up to 10Hz
over the hour from 3pm to 4pm.

The time-specification in its simplest form may be in the form 'hh:mm'
or 'hh:mm:ss'.  This counts as an absolute time.

Another form of time-specification is 'NOW' which indicates the time
at which sbagen was started.  This is useful for sequence-files that
are intended to play from the time that they are run, rather than
always at the same time of day.  'NOW' is another absolute time.

The other form of time-spec is a relative time, either '+hh:mm' or
'+hh:mm:ss'.  This, if used alone, is taken relative to the
last-mentioned absolute time, for example the three relative times
below are all relative to 'NOW', the last-mentioned absolute time:

  NOW+00:10 theta4
  +00:20    off
  +00:30    theta4
  +00:40    off

Any number of relative times may also be used with an absolute time,
to modify it, for example 'NOW+04:00+01:12:04' or '12:00+00:00:20'.


3.1.4: Block definition lines
-----------------------------

This introduces a set of lines, which can then be later used like a
'rubber stamp' to repeat the same set of lines at specific times
throughout the sequence.  This takes the form:

  <block-name>: {
    <time-sequence-line>
    ::
  }

For example:

  theta-bursts: {
    +00:00 theta4
    +00:05 off
    +00:10 theta4
    +00:15 off
  }

A block definition associates a name with a group of time-sequence
lines.  All the time-specs on these lines should be relative, because
when the block is called on, all the times will be combined with the
time-spec from the calling line.

For example, using the above definition:

  NOW theta-bursts

expands to:

  NOW+00:00 theta4
  NOW+00:05 off
  NOW+00:10 theta4
  NOW+00:15 off

Or for another example:

  +01:00 theta-bursts

expands to:

  +01:00+00:00 theta4
  +01:00+00:05 off
  +01:00+00:10 theta4
  +01:00+00:15 off

You can also use block-names within other blocks.  For example:

  two-burst-sets: {
    +0:00 theta-bursts
    +1:00 theta-bursts
  }

Saying:

  NOW two-burst-sets

expands to:

  NOW+0:00 theta-bursts
  NOW+1:00 theta-bursts

which expands to:  

  NOW+0:00+00:00 theta4
  NOW+0:00+00:05 off
  NOW+0:00+00:10 theta4
  NOW+0:00+00:15 off
  NOW+1:00+00:00 theta4
  NOW+1:00+00:05 off
  NOW+1:00+00:10 theta4
  NOW+1:00+00:15 off

By putting little sequences into blocks like this, it makes it easier
to later build up a sequence from fixed parts.


3.2: Multiple sequence files
----------------------------

If multiple files are specified on the command-line, then they are all
loaded together as a single sequence.  This may be useful if you want
to keep tone-set and block definitions separate from the sequences
themselves.

Note that putting two different full sequences together typically
won't work unless they were designed to work together.


4: Predefined sequences
----------------------

SBaGenX comes with several predefined sequences that you can use without
having to create your own sequence files. These are accessed using
the -p option followed by the sequence name and any required parameters.

The most commonly used predefined sequences are:

1. **drop** - A sequence that gradually drops from a higher frequency to
   a lower one, useful for meditation and relaxation. Example:
   `sbagenx -p drop 00ds+`

2. **slide** - Similar to drop but with a smoother transition between
   frequencies.
   Example: `sbagenx -p slide 00ds+`

3. **sigmoid** - Similar to drop, but in `s` mode the beat/pulse follows a
   sigmoid curve over the drop period.  Example:
   `sbagenx -p sigmoid 00ds+:l=0.125:h=0:S=2`

4. **curve** - Uses expressions from a `.sbgf` file to define beat/pulse
   (and optionally carrier) over time.  Example:
   `sbagenx -p curve <file.sbgf> 00ls:l=0.2:h=0`

These predefined sequences accept various parameters to customize their
behavior.
See sections 2.6, 2.7, 2.10 and 2.11 for detailed information on
existing parameters and options.  The sigmoid sequence accepts the same
drop-style format plus optional shape parameters `:l=<value>` and
`:h=<value>` and optional start-beat `:S=<value>` (order does not
matter), with defaults `l=0.125`, `h=0`, and `S=10.0`.

For `-p sigmoid ...s...`, the beat/pulse frequency follows:

  f(t) = a * tanh( l * (t - D/2 - h) ) + b

Where:
  t = time in minutes from start of the drop
  D = drop duration in minutes (default 30)
  l, h = user shape parameters

Boundary conditions:
  f(0) = S (default 10.0)
  f(D) = target from target spec, same target rules as `-p drop`

`a` and `b` are solved automatically from those boundary conditions:
  a = (f(D) - f(0)) /
      ( tanh( l * ( D/2 - h) ) - tanh( l * (-D/2 - h) ) )
  b = f(0) - a * tanh( l * (-D/2 - h) )

You can also mix these predefined sequences with background sounds:
   Example: `sbagenx -m river1.ogg -p drop 00ds+ mix/99`

The program also includes several example sequence files (prog-*.sbg)
and tone-set files (ts-*.sbg) that you can use as templates for
creating your own sequences.


5: Conclusion
-------------

Hopefully this should be enough information to allow you to design and
write your own sequences now.  Playing around with the example
sequences (the prog-* files) will also help.

Happy experimenting !!



Appendix-A: User-defined waveforms
----------------------------------

Note: This feature is not related to the functionality of adding
waveforms directly to tone sets.

This feature started as an older experimental idea, but in SBaGenX it
is now implemented and usable.  The `waveNN` data is transformed into a
smooth 0-100% unipolar envelope over one beat cycle and applied as the
shared amplitude envelope of binaural tones generated from it.
  
  The purest form of binaural beat synthesis is based on two sine waves
  of slightly differing frequency.  The interference between these two
  within the brain generates a resultant wave with a slow modulation.
  Assuming the interference within the brain is similar to normal wave
  interference, then the resultant amplitude wave is shaped by what
  looks like a full-wave rectified sine wave (like the +ve half of a
  sine wave repeated endlessly).
  
  There is a line of thinking that suggests that if we replace this
  amplitude envelope with one based on the shape of a measured
  brain-wave, then we should be able to reproduce that brain-wave in the
  brain -- i.e. we play a measured alpha wave to entrain the brain
  precisely to an alpha wave.  Does this work ?  I really don't know.
  
  This is how it is used in the sequence-file.  You provide the shape of
  the brain-wave to the program in a waveform definition line:
  
    wave<2-digits>: <list of samples>
  
  There can be at most 100 waveforms defined in a sequence, numbered 00
  to 99.  For example:
  
    wave00: 0 5.5 10 8 10 7.2 4.7 2 
  
  The samples are values from the waveform shape taken at regular
  intervals over one cycle.  They can be in any units you wish --
  floating point numbers, negative, whatever -- and there may be as many
  samples as you wish (currently only limited by line length).  They
  will be rescaled to fit between the amplitudes of 0% and 100% and
  smoothed using a sin(x)/x function.  See here for more details on the
  smoothing used:
  
    http://www-ccrma.stanford.edu/~jos/resample/
  
  This waveform now becomes the amplitude envelope of any binaural tones
  generated from it:
  
    wave00:200+4/50
  
  This is just the same as 200+4/50, except it uses the new waveform for
  the amplitude envelope.
  
  That's all you need to know to be able to use it.  All you have to do
  now (according to the theory, at least) is to go and measure some
  waveforms -- off the 'net if like most of us you don't have an EEG --
  feed them into waveNN: lines, and then play them at appropriate
  beat-frequencies.
  
  Implementation note (SBaGenX):

  The custom waveform is treated as an amplitude envelope over one beat
  cycle.  It is scaled to 0-100%, smoothed by periodic sinc
  interpolation, and then applied at the beat rate while the underlying
  left/right carriers are still generated at `carrier +/- beat/2`.

  This means that `waveNN` remains a legacy "shape import" feature rather
  than a literal envelope authoring format.  In particular, a numeric input
  sample of `0` does not necessarily end up at zero amplitude after the
  periodic sinc interpolation plus global min/max normalization.

  If you want a more intuitive literal envelope where `0` stays `0`, use
  `customNN` instead:

    custom<2-digits>: [e=<0..3>] <list of non-negative samples>

  `customNN` samples are interpreted as a unipolar envelope, scaled only by
  their maximum value and interpolated around the cycle using:

    e=0 hard / step
    e=1 linear (default)
    e=2 smoothstep
    e=3 smootherstep

  For example:

    custom00: e=2 0 0.1 0.9 1 1 0.9 0.1 0
    custom00:400@2/20

  This creates a 400Hz isochronic tone pulsing at 2Hz with a custom literal
  envelope.  The same `customNN:` prefix may also be used with binaural
  tones.  Unlike `waveNN`, `customNN` is intended for envelope design, not
  arbitrary imported waveform normalization.  A rendered reference plot is
  shipped as `assets/example-plot-iso-cycle-custom00-literal.png`.

  The historical derivation below is retained for reference:
  
  > Let's say we have a brain-wave waveform of f(T) (T is theta),
  > expressed as a fourier series (using complex maths because it's
  > easier):
  > 
  >   f(T) = sigma(n= -inf to +inf) ( Cn . e^(inT) )
  > 
  > What we want to end up with is a resultant function something like
  > this:
  > 
  >   g(t) = sigma(n= -inf to +inf) ( Cn . e^(inbt) . 2 cos nat )
  > 
  > where 'a' is the resultant carrier frequency (in rad/s here), and 'b'
  > is the beat frequency.  This means that the phase information is
  > connected with the beat waveform, and the phase of the carriers are
  > fixed.  The factor 2 comes in handy lower down:
  > 
  >   g(t) = sigma(n= -inf to +inf) ( Cn . e^(inbt) . (e^(inat) + e^(-inat)) )
  > 
  >   g(t) = sigma(n= -inf to +inf) ( Cn . e^(in(b+a)t) + Cn . e^(in(b-a)t) )
  > 
  >   g(t) = f((b+a)t) + f((b-a)t)
  > 
  > So we just need to play the original waveform at frequencies (b+a) and
  > (b-a).  But 'b' will typically be small (1-30Hz), and 'a' will be
  > large (100-1500Hz).  So (b-a) is negative, which means playing the
  > sample backwards.
  > 
  > So if we play the required brainwave forwards at the higher
  > frequency, and backwards at the lower frequency, it should all come
  > out with what we want.
  
  However, with my experiments I don't get the expected resultant
  amplitude envelope.  I get bits of it, but not a perfect result.
  What's going on, I don't know.  Anyway, if you're up for experimenting
  with this, at least the code is there now for you to play with and
  modify.  If you make any progress, let me have the improved code, and
  I'll include it in the next release.


Appendix-B: Plot backend and Windows runtime notes
--------------------------------------------------

Plot backend environment overrides:

  SBAGENX_PLOT_BACKEND=internal|python|cairo|external
    internal  force built-in C renderer
    python    force external Python/Cairo backend (error if unavailable)
  SBAGENX_PLOT_PYTHON=<path>
    absolute path to Python executable to use for plotting
  SBAGENX_PLOT_SCRIPT=<path>
    absolute path to sbagenx_plot.py

Windows build/runtime bundling notes:

  windows-build-sbagenx.sh now treats Python+Cairo runtimes as required.
  It bundles both architectures and fails the build if either one cannot
  be prepared.

  Runtime sources are checked in this order:
    1) pre-populated runtime trees:
       libs/windows-win32-python-runtime/
       libs/windows-win64-python-runtime/
    2) archives (auto-extracted):
       libs/windows-win32-python-runtime.zip
       libs/windows-win64-python-runtime.zip
       (or paths via SBAGENX_WIN32_PY_RUNTIME_ARCHIVE /
        SBAGENX_WIN64_PY_RUNTIME_ARCHIVE)
    3) official auto-prep:
       - CPython embeddable package
       - matching pycairo wheel
       defaults:
         SBAGENX_PY_EMBED_VERSION=3.13.12
         SBAGENX_PYCAIRO_VERSION=1.29.0

  Additional controls:
    SBAGENX_AUTO_PREPARE_PY_RUNTIME=1|0  (default 1)
    SBAGENX_REQUIRE_PY_RUNTIME=1|0       (default 1)
    SBAGENX_WIN32_PY_RUNTIME_URL
    SBAGENX_WIN64_PY_RUNTIME_URL
